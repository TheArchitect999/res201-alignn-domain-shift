## Paper-level guardrails

- Follow the brief-specified order: Introduction, Methods, Results I (oxide), Results II (nitride), Results III (direct comparison), Results IV (embedding analysis), Discussion, Conclusion, References.
- Keep nitride `N <= 200` framed as inert fine-tuning.
- Keep nitride `N=50` transfer benefit framed as initialization advantage, not adaptation gain.
- Use `last_alignn_pool` as the main embedding layer in text and figures.
- Do not imply from-scratch comparisons exist at `N=10`, `100`, `200`, or `1000`.
- The Introduction must contain only background, motivation, and objective framing. Do not place result tables or MAE values in the Introduction.
- Do not describe the pretrained checkpoint as "oxide-pretrained". Use "pretrained formation-energy ALIGNN model" or "pretrained ALIGNN model". "Oxide-reference region" is acceptable only in embedding-distance contexts.

## Prose-drafting guardrails

These five rules apply when converting blueprint rows into actual prose. They are enforced at draft time, not at blueprint time.

1. **Keep the Introduction result-free in actual prose.** The Introduction rows say "no result tables or MAE values" â€” this extends to inline claims too. Do not write sentences like "the model achieves X eV/atom on oxide" in the Introduction. Frame only the question, the approach, and the paper map.

2. **Treat subsection blueprint rows as the primary drafting source.** Each row defines the claim, figures, tables, and canonical numbers for that subsection. Draft from the row, not from memory or re-reading the raw data files. If the row's canonical numbers are insufficient, escalate to `canonical_numbers_v2.csv` â€” do not pull values from source CSVs directly.

3. **Repeat the nitride N=50 caveat every time `CN_TRANSFER_BENEFIT_NITRIDE_N50` appears in text.** The required phrasing is: "pretrained-initialization advantage over scratch, not fine-tuning adaptation". Do not omit or abbreviate this caveat even in passing references.

4. **Keep `last_alignn_pool` as the only main-text embedding layer.** If a draft sentence references `pre_head` or `last_gcn_pool` in the main body, move it to the appendix. The phrase "embedding analysis" in main text always means `last_alignn_pool` unless the appendix is explicitly being introduced.

5. **Do not let Results III become long.** Results III is a compare-not-replay section. Each subsection (rows 10, 11, 12) should be shorter than its corresponding Results I or Results II counterpart. If Results III prose exceeds the combined length of its source subsections, it has drifted into re-explanation â€” cut it back.

## Results III â€” compare-not-replay rule

**This rule applies to all Results III subsections (rows 10â€“12):**

Results III must synthesize and directly contrast evidence already presented in Results I and II. It must NOT re-explain either family's standalone story at full length. The preferred structure for each Results III subsection is:

1. One opening synthesis sentence naming the comparison axis.
2. One comparative paragraph that places oxide and nitride side by side on that axis.
3. One direct-comparison figure or table anchor.
4. One closing interpretation sentence on what the contrast reveals.

Long restatement of oxide evidence (from Results I) or nitride evidence (from Results II) is not permitted inside Results III. Treat Results III as a cross-examination layer, not a replay layer.
