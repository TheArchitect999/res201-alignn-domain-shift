## Results III. Direct oxide–nitride comparison

Results I and Results II established the oxide and nitride arms separately. This section places the two families on a single comparative footing using three axes that together characterize the behavioral domain-shift penalty: the zero-shot starting point, the fine-tuning response across training-set sizes, and the transfer benefit of pretrained initialization over from-scratch training. All comparisons in this section use the same pretrained formation-energy ALIGNN checkpoint [CITE: ALIGNN_PAPER], the same JARVIS `dft_3d` split logic [CITE: JARVIS_INFRA], the same canonical hyperparameter set (`epochs = 50`, `lr = 1e-4`, `batch = 16`), and the same fixed oxide and nitride test sets (`1484` and `242` structures, respectively). Parity-plot MAE values reported here are on-figure values computed from seed-averaged predictions and therefore differ slightly from the summary MAE values in Table TAB_S1_FT_SUMMARY_BY_N, which are computed as per-seed MAE averages.

### III.A. Zero-shot family gap

**What is being compared.** We evaluate a single pretrained formation-energy ALIGNN checkpoint, with no target-family training, on the oxide test set (`1484` structures) and on the nitride test set (`242` structures). This isolates the behavioral starting point of transfer before any target-family signal has been seen.

**What the figure shows.** Figure FIG_ZS_COMPARISON reports a same-checkpoint zero-shot MAE of `0.0342 eV/atom` on oxides and `0.0695 eV/atom` on nitrides. Nitride zero-shot MAE exceeds oxide zero-shot MAE by `0.0354 eV/atom` and is `2.03×` the oxide value.

**What pattern is consistent.** The direction of the gap is consistent with the project framing that treats oxides as an in-distribution family relative to the broad JARVIS-trained checkpoint and nitrides as a chemical-family out-of-distribution test [CITE: PROJECT_BRIEF]. The comparison is not attributable to a different checkpoint or a different evaluation protocol: both families are evaluated with the same pretrained model on fixed held-out splits produced by the canonical split logic. The two test sets differ in size (`1484` oxides and `242` nitrides), so the sampling uncertainty on each family's zero-shot MAE is not identical; this may affect how tightly each zero-shot value is pinned, but it does not change the direction of the observed gap.

**What interpretation is justified.** The zero-shot comparison establishes a behavioral domain-shift penalty of approximately a factor of two in formation-energy MAE before any target-family fine-tuning. It characterizes the starting point from which each family's adaptation curve will be measured in Sections III.B and III.C.

**What is uncertain.** This comparison is a behavioral observation, not a mechanism claim. The zero-shot MAE gap is compatible with several non-exclusive causes — distributional differences in bond chemistry, differences in available pretraining coverage, or differences in the intrinsic hardness of formation-energy prediction between the two families — and no causal attribution is made here. Section IV introduces a correlational geometric counterpart that is consistent with the gap but does not explain it.

### III.B. Differential fine-tuning response across training-set sizes

**What is being compared.** We fine-tune the same pretrained checkpoint on each family at `N ∈ {10, 50, 100, 200, 500, 1000}` labeled structures, using multiple seeds. For each family and each `N`, we report mean test MAE and the mean best epoch across seeds. The mean best epoch is the per-seed epoch at which validation loss reached its minimum, averaged across seeds; it is a direct behavioral indicator of whether the model has entered a multi-epoch optimization regime or remained at an early-checkpoint fixed point.

**What the figure shows.** The paired learning-curve panels (FIG_S1_LC_OXIDE and FIG_S1_LC_NITRIDE) and the accompanying summary table (TAB_S1_FT_SUMMARY_BY_N) reveal three coupled patterns. First, at every `N`, nitride mean MAE exceeds oxide mean MAE; the family gap in mean fine-tuned MAE ranges from `0.0457 eV/atom` at `N = 10` to `0.1257 eV/atom` at `N = 100`. Second, oxide mean best epoch climbs from `1.0` at `N = 10` to `18.5` at `N = 50` and then to `20.0`, `39.0`, `39.0`, and `35.5` at `N = 100`, `200`, `500`, and `1000`. Third, nitride mean best epoch remains pinned at `1.0` across `N = 10`, `50`, `100`, and `200`, and only departs from this pinned value at `N = 500` (`40.5`) and `N = 1000` (`45.0`). No nitride fine-tuning row beats nitride zero-shot MAE; the best genuinely adapted nitride configuration at `N = 1000` reaches `0.0907 eV/atom`, still above the nitride zero-shot value of `0.0695 eV/atom`.

**What pattern is consistent.** The family contrast is not only that nitride MAE is uniformly higher. It is that oxide begins genuine multi-epoch optimization by `N = 50`, whereas nitride remains operationally inert through `N = 200` in the sense that validation loss is minimized at the first epoch and does not improve over the fifty-epoch run. The mean best epoch distinguishes these regimes more reliably than a single MAE value, because two models can have similar MAE for very different reasons — one because it has been optimized to a stable fit, the other because it has not yet moved away from its initialization.

**What interpretation is justified.** The nitride low-`N` rows should be read as reports of the pretrained checkpoint at the first fine-tuning epoch, not as successful low-data adaptation. The clean cross-family comparison of adapted fine-tuning begins at `N = 500`, where both families show mean best epochs well beyond `1.0`. Even at that size, and at `N = 1000`, the family gap persists: the oxide endpoint is close to its zero-shot MAE while the nitride endpoint remains above nitride zero-shot MAE. This is consistent with a picture in which the chemical-family shift is felt not only in the starting error but in how readily the same optimization protocol converts labeled data into adaptation.

**What is uncertain.** We cannot rule out that a different hyperparameter schedule — for example, a longer training budget, a different learning-rate schedule, or a more aggressive early-layer unfreezing policy — would push nitride fine-tuning into genuine adaptation at lower `N`. The canonical namespace used here (`epochs = 50`, `lr = 1e-4`, `batch = 16`) is the project-fixed protocol and not the result of a per-family tuning search. No claim is made here about alternative hyperparameter settings; the behavioral pattern described is specific to the canonical protocol.

### III.C. Transfer-benefit contrast: pretrained versus from-scratch

**What is being compared.** From-scratch baselines were trained for both families at `N = 50` and `N = 500`. The transfer benefit is defined as the difference between the from-scratch test MAE and the fine-tuned test MAE at the same `N`. No transfer-benefit claim is made at `N = 10`, `N = 100`, `N = 200`, or `N = 1000`, because no from-scratch baseline exists at those sizes in the canonical namespace.

**What the figure shows.** Figure FIG_TRANSFER_BENEFIT reports the following. For oxides, the from-scratch MAE is `0.5561 eV/atom` at `N = 50` and `0.2643 eV/atom` at `N = 500`; the fine-tuned MAE is `0.0523 eV/atom` and `0.0430 eV/atom`, yielding transfer benefits of `0.5038 eV/atom` and `0.2214 eV/atom`, respectively. For nitrides, the from-scratch MAE is `0.6914 eV/atom` at `N = 50` and `0.3683 eV/atom` at `N = 500`; the fine-tuned MAE is `0.1173 eV/atom` and `0.0977 eV/atom`, yielding transfer benefits of `0.5741 eV/atom` and `0.2706 eV/atom`. Transfer-benefit bars are numerically larger for nitrides than for oxides at both tested sizes.

**Critical interpretation caveats, stated up front.** Two properties of this comparison must be established before any cross-family ranking is read from the bar heights.

1. **Numerically taller nitride bars do not indicate more efficient transfer on nitrides.** At both `N = 50` and `N = 500`, the nitride from-scratch anchor is weaker and the nitride fine-tuned endpoint is worse in absolute MAE terms than the corresponding oxide quantities. The arithmetic benefit is therefore inflated by a worse scratch baseline, not by better adaptation, and bar-height ordering between families cannot be used as a measure of transfer efficiency.
2. **The nitride `N = 50` bar is pretrained-initialization advantage over scratch, not low-data adaptation.** The nitride mean best epoch at `N = 50` is `1.0`, so the fine-tuned model at that size has not moved meaningfully away from the pretrained checkpoint. The `N = 50` nitride bar measures the advantage of starting from that pretrained state instead of from random initialization; it does not measure successful target-family fine-tuning. Under the canonical protocol, the first clean cross-family comparison of adaptation benefit is at `N = 500`, where both families show mean best epochs well above `1.0`.
3. **Scope is limited to `N = 50` and `N = 500`.** No from-scratch baseline exists at other `N`, so no transfer-benefit statement is made elsewhere on the curve.

**What pattern is consistent, with the caveats in place.** With those caveats fixed, two observations remain. Pretrained initialization outperforms random initialization in both families at both tested sizes, and the advantage is larger at `N = 50` than at `N = 500` in both families. This is qualitatively consistent with the general transfer-learning picture in which pretrained models retain value most strongly in the low-data regime [CITE: KIM2024; LEE_ASAHI2021].

**What interpretation is justified.** Pretraining is valuable for nitrides at the two scratch-tested sizes in the specific sense of outperforming random initialization by a large margin. This is a meaningful practical finding for small-data materials workflows: in the low-data regime, starting from the pretrained formation-energy checkpoint is much better than training an ALIGNN from scratch on the target family, even though — as III.B shows — the nitride family still does not benefit from adaptation at low `N` and does not close the family gap at high `N`.

**What is uncertain.** The transfer-benefit comparison reported here is specific to the canonical hyperparameter set, the canonical JARVIS splits, and the two tested scratch sizes. No extrapolation is made to intermediate `N`, to other optimization schedules, or to other evaluation protocols.

### III.D. Endpoint parity comparison

**What is being compared.** As a visual endpoint diagnostic, we examine parity plots at the highest tested `N` for both families (FIG_S1_PARITY_OXIDE_N1000 and FIG_S1_PARITY_NITRIDE_N1000), where both families have entered the genuinely adapted regime (oxide mean best epoch `35.5`; nitride mean best epoch `45.0`).

**What the figure shows.** On-figure MAE values are `0.0383 eV/atom` for oxides and `0.0829 eV/atom` for nitrides. The oxide parity scatter is visually tight along the diagonal across the population. The nitride parity scatter shows larger dispersion, including tail structures at both the low and high ends of the formation-energy range.

**What interpretation is justified.** Even when oxide and nitride fine-tuning are matched on the canonical hyperparameter set and are both in the adapted regime, the parity gap persists. This is consistent with the behavioral pattern in III.B, in which nitride fine-tuning reduces optimization inertness but does not close the family error gap.

**What is uncertain.** Parity dispersion is a visual indicator, not a compositional breakdown. A finer-grained decomposition of the nitride residual by composition, coordination environment, or structural prototype would strengthen the interpretation but is beyond the scope of this section.

### III.E. Bridge to Results IV

Across zero-shot evaluation, fine-tuning across `N`, transfer-benefit at the two scratch-tested sizes, and high-`N` parity, a single comparative picture holds: pretraining helps in both families, but it does not close the family gap for nitrides under the canonical protocol. Section IV therefore asks a different question than Section III: does the frozen pretrained representation that produces these outputs contain geometric structure consistent with the observed penalty?

## Results IV. Embedding analysis and interpretation of domain shift

Section III characterizes the behavioral penalty using test MAE, learning-curve shape, adaptation timing, and transfer-benefit arithmetic. Those are outputs of the model. Section IV examines the internal representation that produces them: the penultimate pooled features of the pretrained ALIGNN checkpoint, extracted with the model frozen and no target-family training. The goal of this section is not to replace the behavioral comparison with an embedding-based one, but to test whether the frozen representation shows structure that is geometrically consistent with the behavioral penalty already established. Every quantitative claim in Section IV is computed in the raw 256-dimensional `last_alignn_pool` space; PCA, t-SNE, and UMAP panels are used only as descriptive visual support and are not the basis of any numerical claim.

### IV.A. Implementation of the embedding analysis

Embeddings are extracted from the layer denoted `last_alignn_pool`, which is the pooled graph-level feature immediately after the ALIGNN stack and before the formation-energy regression head. For each structure in the analysis set, the embedding is a raw 256-dimensional vector. **All statistical claims in this section are computed in the raw 256-dimensional space.** Two-dimensional projections (PCA, t-SNE, UMAP) appear as descriptive figures only. Their axes, inter-cluster distances, cluster sizes, and apparent densities are not physically metric quantities and are not used to make numerical claims; t-SNE and UMAP in particular preserve aspects of local neighborhood structure but do not preserve global inter-cluster geometry [CITE: WEEK2_ASSIGNMENT]. The PCA projection used in the main figure explains `18.13%` of variance on PC1 and `9.47%` on PC2 (`27.60%` cumulative) when fit on a balanced pool of `4092` structures, and is therefore informative but lossy as a summary of the 256-D geometry.

The family-separation analysis uses the fixed test set of `1726` structures (`1484` oxides and `242` nitrides). The distance–error analysis uses the full nitride test set (`242` structures) and an oxide reference pool of `13507` oxide train+val structures. For each nitride test structure, we compute the mean Euclidean distance from its raw 256-D `last_alignn_pool` embedding to its five nearest oxide-reference embeddings; this is the "5NN oxide-reference distance" used throughout IV.C. Statistical tests report 95% confidence intervals (bootstrap) and FDR-adjusted `q`-values.

### IV.B. Family separation in the frozen pretrained representation

**What is being compared.** We characterize whether oxides and nitrides occupy distinguishable regions of the frozen `last_alignn_pool` representation, and whether the two regions differ in cohesion. All quantitative claims in this subsection are based on raw 256-D metrics; the PCA, t-SNE, and UMAP panels are included as descriptive visual support only, and are not used to support any compactness, asymmetry, or inter-family distance statement.

**What the raw-space metrics show.** Computed in the raw 256-D `last_alignn_pool` embedding space across the fixed test set, the family-separation metrics summarized in Table TAB_EA_FAMILY_SEPARATION are as follows. The overall silhouette score is `0.2392` (95% CI `0.2332`–`0.2456`); the per-family silhouettes are `0.2546` for oxides (CI `0.2476`–`0.2617`) and `0.1453` for nitrides (CI `0.1316`–`0.1582`). The Davies–Bouldin index is `1.8290` (CI `1.7340`–`1.9071`). Local 15-nearest-neighbor family purity is `0.9655` overall (CI `0.9603`–`0.9708`), with oxide neighborhoods at `0.9872` (CI `0.9832`–`0.9906`) and nitride neighborhoods at `0.8331` (CI `0.7978`–`0.8645`). A logistic regression trained to recover family labels from the frozen embeddings achieves an AUC of `0.9994` (CI `0.9984`–`0.9999`).

**What pattern is consistent.** Two raw-space patterns emerge. First, family labels are almost perfectly recoverable from the frozen 256-D embeddings (AUC `0.9994`), so the pretrained representation encodes chemistry-level information that distinguishes oxides from nitrides even without any supervision on that distinction. Second, the two families are not symmetric in the raw space: by per-family silhouette (`0.2546` vs `0.1453`) and by per-family 15-NN family purity (`0.9872` vs `0.8331`), the nitride region is distinguishable from oxides but internally less organized than the oxide region. The PCA, t-SNE, and UMAP panels (FIG_EA_6A_PCA, FIG_EA_6B_TSNE, FIG_EA_6C_UMAP) are broadly consistent with the raw-space pattern and are included as descriptive visual support for how that structure appears under three standard projection methods. No claim about family compactness, family asymmetry, or inter-family distance in Section IV is grounded on the projections themselves.

**What interpretation is justified.** The frozen pretrained representation already carries family-level structure before any target-family training, and the per-family asymmetry in raw-space cohesion is aligned with the direction of the behavioral asymmetry established in Section III. Raw-space metrics and behavioral MAE curves are consistent; they describe the same contrast from two different measurement surfaces.

**What is uncertain.** The logistic-regression AUC quantifies family-label recoverability from the frozen embeddings, not the mechanism by which formation-energy prediction errors arise; a near-perfect family AUC is compatible with many downstream error patterns. The silhouette, Davies–Bouldin, 15-NN purity, and logistic-AUC values are raw-space geometric descriptors; they do not quantify how much of the behavioral MAE gap is attributable to representation structure as opposed to label distribution, prediction-head calibration, or other non-geometric factors. The PCA, t-SNE, and UMAP panels are descriptive only; their axes, inter-cluster distances, cluster sizes, and apparent densities are not metric quantities in the original 256-D space, and no numerical claim in this section rests on them.

### IV.C. Error-linked distance: nitride zero-shot error versus oxide-reference distance

**What is being compared.** Within the `242` nitride test structures, we ask whether the nitrides that the pretrained model predicts poorly (large absolute zero-shot error) lie farther in the raw 256-D `last_alignn_pool` space from the oxide-reference pool than the nitrides the model predicts well. We use two complementary views: a hard-versus-easy tail contrast, and a full-sample continuous association.

**What the figure and table show.** Table TAB_EA_DISTANCE_ERROR_STATS and the two panels FIG_EA_6D_BOXPLOT and FIG_EA_6D_SCATTER summarize the analysis. Defining "hard" and "easy" nitrides as the top and bottom `20%` by absolute zero-shot error (`49` structures each), the mean 5NN oxide-reference distance is `4.5988` for the hard group and `3.7821` for the easy group. The hard-minus-easy mean gap is `0.8168` (95% CI `0.4746`–`1.1597`, FDR `q = 0.000180`), and the hard-minus-easy median gap is `0.8729` (95% CI `0.4161`–`1.2864`, FDR `q = 0.000300`). Across the full set of `242` nitrides, the Spearman rank correlation between 5NN oxide-reference distance and absolute zero-shot error is `0.3428` (95% CI `0.2214`–`0.4597`, FDR `q = 0.000129`), and the Pearson correlation is `0.2770` (95% CI `0.1741`–`0.3890`, FDR `q = 0.000129`).

**What pattern is consistent.** Both the tail-group contrast and the full-sample association point in the same direction: nitrides located farther from the oxide-reference region in the frozen pretrained space tend to have larger zero-shot errors. The boxplot view captures this as a clear effect-size difference at the tails, and the scatter view shows that the relationship is not only a property of extreme cases; it extends across the full nitride test population as a moderate positive monotonic and linear association.

**What interpretation is justified.** The 5NN oxide-reference distance is a correlational geometric indicator of nitride prediction difficulty. It gives the embedding analysis its distinctive content relative to Section III: test MAE and learning curves describe the behavioral penalty at the family level, but they do not localize relative difficulty to any representation-space feature; the distance–error association co-varies with zero-shot error across the nitride population and therefore provides a within-family geometric correlate of that difficulty.

**What is uncertain.** The relationship is correlational and non-deterministic. The hard and easy groups overlap substantially in distance, the Spearman rank correlation is moderate (`0.3428`), and the association is reported for absolute zero-shot error only — not for fine-tuned error, where the picture may differ. The analysis does not claim that distance from the oxide-reference pool causes prediction error, that it is the only geometric factor associated with error, or that it applies symmetrically to oxides; the oxide-reference pool is an operational reference region defined from the heavier-represented family in the training data, not evidence that the pretrained checkpoint is oxide-exclusive.

### IV.D. What the embedding analysis adds beyond MAE and learning curves

The MAE tables and learning curves in Sections III.A–III.D establish the domain-shift penalty as a behavioral property of the model: nitride zero-shot error is about `2×` oxide zero-shot error, oxide optimization begins at `N = 50` while nitride optimization is delayed to `N = 500`, and the nitride family gap persists even when both families are in the adapted regime. Those results answer the question "how large is the penalty, and when does it appear?".

The embedding analysis in Sections IV.B and IV.C answers a different question: "does the frozen pretrained representation, examined before any target-family training, already show structure consistent with the observed penalty?" It contributes three pieces of information that MAE tables and learning curves do not. First, it shows that family structure is already present in the frozen representation to the point where a linear probe recovers family labels almost perfectly (AUC `0.9994`), bringing the representation itself — and not only the downstream regression head — into view as a geometric correlate of the domain-shift picture. Second, it shows that the family structure is asymmetric in raw space: by every per-family raw-space metric considered, the oxide region is more internally organized than the nitride region, and that asymmetry in raw-space cohesion aligns with the direction of the behavioral asymmetry. Third, it provides a within-family geometric indicator — 5NN oxide-reference distance — that co-varies with zero-shot error across the full nitride population, so the embedding analysis can localize relative difficulty to individual structures in a way that aggregate MAE cannot.

None of these additions replaces the behavioral evidence, and none demonstrates causality. What the embedding analysis adds is a representation-space counterpart to the behavioral pattern, which makes the domain-shift interpretation geometrically legible without upgrading it to a mechanism claim.

### IV.E. Synthesis of the geometric and behavioral evidence

Taken together, Sections III and IV describe a consistent picture under the canonical protocol. The pretrained formation-energy ALIGNN checkpoint starts from a meaningfully higher zero-shot MAE on nitrides than on oxides, adapts slowly on nitrides relative to oxides across `N`, and does not close the family gap even at `N = 1000`. In the frozen representation from which those outputs are computed, oxides and nitrides occupy family-distinguishable regions in raw 256-D space, the nitride region is less cohesive than the oxide region by every per-family raw-space metric considered, and, within nitrides, zero-shot error co-varies with distance from the oxide-reference region. The behavioral penalty and the representation-space geometry are consistent, but the consistency is correlational, not causal: the frozen representation is the setting in which the penalty is measured, not a proven source of it. The discussion section develops the practical implications of this combined picture for small-data transfer in materials property prediction.
