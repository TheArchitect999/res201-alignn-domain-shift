## 4. Discussion

### 4.1 What the oxide control establishes, in brief

The oxide arm was designed as an in-distribution control against which the nitride arm's domain-shift penalty could be measured. Read on its own terms, it does three independently useful things. It quantifies the value of pretrained initialization against the natural counterfactual of training the same architecture from scratch on the same data under the same protocol. It shows that, on a chemistry-aligned target, the zero-shot pretrained checkpoint is already strong enough that the canonical fine-tuning protocol cannot improve on it across the tested data range. And it provides a representation-level view in which oxides occupy a cohesive, locally pure region of the pretrained feature space that is consistent with that behavioural picture. The rest of this Discussion develops each of these in order, keeps literature context separate from our findings, and closes with limitations and future work.

### 4.2 What pretraining helps with on the in-distribution task

Prior work on transfer learning for crystal graph neural networks establishes that reusing pretrained representations is most effective when the target is chemically close to the data regime that supported pretraining, and that the largest per-sample benefit is typically observed in the low-data regime [CITE: Lee & Asahi 2021; Kim et al. 2024]. Pretrained graph-neural networks in materials informatics more broadly [CITE: Xie & Grossman 2018 — CGCNN; Choudhary & DeCost 2021 — ALIGNN; Choudhary et al. 2020 — JARVIS] are commonly used either as zero-shot baselines or as initialization for supervised fine-tuning [CITE: Choudhary et al. 2024 — JARVIS-Leaderboard].

Our oxide evidence speaks to a specific form of that benefit. Under Hyperparameter Set 1 (epochs = 50, learning rate = 1 × 10⁻⁴, batch size = 16, five seeds per configuration) and on the fixed 1,484-structure oxide test set, pretrained initialization delivers a large and consistent advantage over random initialization at the two data sizes where scratch baselines were run. At N = 50 the scratch MAE is 0.5561 ± 0.0523 eV/atom against a fine-tune MAE of 0.0523 ± 0.0148 eV/atom, a gap of 0.5038 eV/atom; at N = 500 the scratch MAE is 0.2643 ± 0.0228 eV/atom against 0.0430 ± 0.0062, a gap of 0.2214 eV/atom. A useful internal calibration sharpens the reading: at N = 500 fine-tuning sits 0.2214 eV/atom below scratch while sitting only 0.0088 eV/atom above the zero-shot benchmark of 0.0342 eV/atom, so the transfer gain relative to a no-pretraining counterfactual is roughly 25× the residual gap to zero-shot. The practical claim that follows is narrow but robust: on a chemistry-aligned target in this data regime, starting from the pretrained formation-energy ALIGNN checkpoint is worth — in labelled-data terms — a great deal more than any fine-tuning decision made downstream of it.

We deliberately do not describe the oxide arm's headline as "fine-tuning improves on zero-shot." It does not under Set 1: every fine-tuning row at N ∈ {10, 50, 100, 200, 500, 1000} sits above the 0.0342 eV/atom benchmark, with positive gaps ranging from 0.0075 to 0.0181 eV/atom. What the oxide arm does show is that the representation the model was already able to deploy zero-shot is, in a quantifiable sense, most of what pretraining is delivering for this family. This is consistent with prior observations that when a pretrained backbone is already strong on the target, additional supervised fine-tuning on a chemistry-aligned task can yield only modest gains [CITE: Choudhary et al. 2024 — JARVIS-Leaderboard].

### 4.3 Why oxide is easier — read as a control, not as a weaker nitride

The oxide arm is the easier arm by construction: oxides sit within a broadly JARVIS-trained representation's operating regime, and the pretrained checkpoint that opens every experiment in this report has therefore already seen oxide-like chemistry during pretraining. Three empirical features of the oxide trajectory are consistent with that positioning. First, fine-tuning enters genuine multi-epoch optimization at N = 50 (mean best epoch 18.5) and stabilizes by N = 200 (mean best epoch 39.0), so the optimizer is willing to move away from initialization rather than staying pinned to the epoch-1 state; the N = 10 row is a lighter exception, flagged `zero_shot_checkpoint_at_low_N` because its mean best epoch of 1.0 shows it did not leave the pretrained initialization in a meaningful way. Second, the mean test MAE recovers monotonically from the small-data penalty at N = 50 (0.0523 eV/atom) back toward the zero-shot benchmark, reaching 0.0417 eV/atom at N = 1000, and cross-seed standard deviation tightens from 0.0111 at N = 10 to 0.0053 at N = 1000. Third, the parity views at the two endpoints are visually similar — on-figure MAE 0.0391 at N = 10 and 0.0383 at N = 1000 — precisely because the pretrained model is already competent on the test population; what changes across N is not the gross shape of the scatter but the optimization depth and cross-seed reproducibility behind each panel.

This is an easier arm, and the report makes that explicit, but "easier" does not mean "uninformative." The oxide evidence is the control against which any conclusion about pretraining value, data efficiency, or domain alignment in this study must be anchored. It is the setting in which the pretrained representation works as advertised, so any deviation elsewhere — the nitride arm's four-step penalty, reported in the companion manuscript — can be read against a concrete reference behaviour rather than against an idealized expectation.

### 4.4 What the embedding view adds on the oxide side

Embedding-level analyses of pretrained crystal GNNs have been used to describe how representations organize along chemical family, composition space, or structural motif [CITE: Choudhary & DeCost 2021; Choudhary 2025]; subsequent out-of-distribution work has argued that the structure of the pretrained representation correlates with where the model generalizes well, while being careful to frame such analyses as **consistent-with** rather than causal evidence [CITE: Omee et al. 2024; Li et al. 2025 — OOD; Hu et al. 2024]. We follow that convention here.

On the oxide side, the `last_alignn_pool` representation places oxides in a cohesive, locally pure region. The 15-nearest-neighbour family purity on oxide test points is 0.9872 — oxide local neighbourhoods in this layer are almost entirely oxide-only — the oxide silhouette is 0.2546, and the family label is recoverable from the raw 256-D embeddings with near-perfect separability (logistic-regression family AUC 0.9994; overall 15-NN purity 0.9655). These raw-space descriptors carry three concrete implications for this report: oxide local neighbourhoods have negligible cross-family contamination; the oxide region is internally more coherent than the nitride region (oxide silhouette 0.2546 vs nitride 0.1453, reported here only to calibrate what "cohesive" means quantitatively); and the pretrained representation organizes the test set along the oxide/non-oxide axis without any supervision on that axis.

Because the oxide arm's scientific weight rests on the behavioural comparison — pretrained initialization beating scratch by roughly 25× the residual gap to zero-shot at N = 500 — the embedding view is best read as a representation-level description that is consistent with that behavioural picture, not as an independent causal explanation of it. Low-dimensional projections (PCA, t-SNE, UMAP) are descriptive support only; visual inter-cluster distances are not quoted as statistical evidence because two-dimensional projections of high-dimensional feature spaces do not preserve global geometry in a physically interpretable way [CITE: van der Maaten & Hinton 2008; McInnes et al. 2018]. The quantitative distance–error analyses that link representation geometry to prediction error are nitride-facing and appear in the combined manuscript; the oxide report forward-references them rather than reproducing them.

### 4.5 Practical implications for small-data oxide prediction

Read conservatively, the oxide result points to two things that are useful for practitioners working on chemistry-aligned targets with limited labelled data. First, in data regimes comparable to ours, the choice of initialization matters much more than the choice of fine-tuning budget. At N = 500 the pretrained initialization is the difference between a test MAE of 0.043 eV/atom and 0.264 eV/atom on the same 1,484-structure test population; the additional fine-tuning budget between N = 500 and N = 1000 is worth only 0.0013 eV/atom on the mean. If a project must choose where to spend computation, spending it on obtaining a good pretrained starting point is a far better investment than spending it on collecting additional labelled oxide data, up to roughly the sample sizes tested here.

Second, because zero-shot is the best observed oxide performance under Set 1, for chemistry-aligned targets in this regime the pretrained checkpoint can be a defensible operational estimator in its own right. Fine-tuning is not wasted — at high N it tightens cross-seed variability substantially (0.0111 → 0.0053 eV/atom) — but it is not necessarily the step that produces the headline accuracy number. A small-data oxide workflow that treats pretrained zero-shot as a first-pass estimator and uses fine-tuning to reduce variance across seeds is coherent with what we observe; a workflow that counts on fine-tuning to beat zero-shot is not. We avoid stronger operational claims because the regime we test is specific: formation energy per atom, JARVIS `dft_3d` oxide splits, Hyperparameter Set 1, six N values up to 1,000.

### 4.6 Limitations

Four limitations of the oxide arm should be kept in view. (i) Scratch baselines exist only at N = 50 and N = 500; we do not infer a continuous scratch learning curve at N = 10, 100, 200, or 1,000, and we do not claim where, if anywhere, scratch would intersect fine-tune or zero-shot at much larger N. (ii) Only six fine-tuning N values were tested, and no row above N = 1,000 is available; the flattening of the oxide learning curve at high N is consistent with convergence toward the zero-shot benchmark from above, but we describe this as flattening, not as a demonstrated saturation. (iii) The claim that zero-shot is the best observed oxide performance is made under Set 1; Set 2 and Set 3 are robustness-only namespaces and whether a different protocol could cross the zero-shot line is out of scope. (iv) The target is formation energy per atom; we do not claim the oxide pattern generalizes to other properties or to other oxide-containing distributions outside the JARVIS `dft_3d` splits used here.

A fifth, softer limitation concerns the embedding section. Raw-space `last_alignn_pool` metrics describe the organization of the pretrained representation; they do not quantify how much of the behavioural MAE gap is attributable to representation geometry as opposed to regression-head calibration, label distribution, or other non-geometric factors. Projection panels are visualization only, and no numerical claim here depends on them.

### 4.7 Future work

Three directions follow naturally from this control arm. First, extending fine-tuning beyond N = 1,000 under Set 1 would clarify whether the oxide learning curve eventually crosses the zero-shot benchmark or continues to flatten against it; the present evidence is consistent with flattening but cannot adjudicate between the two asymptotes. Second, a more granular scratch sweep at N ∈ {10, 100, 200, 1,000} would turn the present two-point pretrained-vs-scratch comparison into a continuous transfer-benefit curve and would allow the data-efficiency advantage to be pinned as a function of N rather than quoted at two sizes. Third, a within-oxide version of the distance–error analysis reported in the nitride arm — correlating intra-family prediction error with pretrained-space geometry — would test whether the correlational geometric indicator generalizes beyond the nitride arm to the in-distribution setting.

## 5. Conclusion

This report examined how a pretrained formation-energy ALIGNN model behaves on an in-distribution oxide regime, with the goal of establishing the control arm of a broader study on chemical-family domain shift. Three results fix that reference condition. Pretrained initialization dominates random initialization at both oxide data sizes where scratch was measured, with transfer benefits of 0.5038 eV/atom at N = 50 and 0.2214 eV/atom at N = 500 — about 25× the residual gap between fine-tuning and the zero-shot benchmark at N = 500. The pretrained zero-shot MAE of 0.0342 eV/atom on 1,484 oxide test structures is the best observed oxide performance under Set 1 in this study, and canonical fine-tuning converges toward that benchmark from above without crossing it. In the pretrained `last_alignn_pool` representation, oxides form a cohesive, locally pure region (15-NN family purity 0.9872; oxide silhouette 0.2546; family AUC 0.9994) that is descriptively consistent with the behavioural picture above.

The practical implication for small-data materials discovery on chemistry-aligned targets is narrow and concrete: in the regime tested, the choice of initialization is the dominant driver of accuracy, fine-tuning is most useful for tightening cross-seed reproducibility rather than for improving the mean, and pretrained zero-shot is a defensible first-pass estimator. These findings set the in-distribution reference condition against which the nitride arm's domain-shift analysis is carried out in the companion report, and they ground the data-efficiency arithmetic of the combined manuscript in a control case where the pretrained representation is known to work as advertised.
