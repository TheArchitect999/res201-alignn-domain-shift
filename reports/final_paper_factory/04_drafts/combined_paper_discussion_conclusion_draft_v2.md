## V. Discussion

### V.A. The comparative answer in one paragraph

The comparative answer this paper supports is narrow: under the canonical protocol on a formation-energy target, a single pretrained ALIGNN checkpoint behaves as transfer learning predicts on the in-distribution oxide arm and only partially as predicted on the out-of-distribution nitride arm, with the gap between the two showing up at every measurement surface examined and with a consistent geometric correlate in the frozen pretrained representation. The oxide arm reproduces the standard chemistry-aligned picture — strong zero-shot, smooth adaptation, a large pretrained-vs-scratch advantage. The nitride arm departs from that picture in a structured way: the starting error is higher, the optimizer does not move off the pretrained checkpoint at low `N` under the canonical protocol, and the family penalty persists in the residual error of the genuinely-adapted models. The frozen representation in which both behaviours are read shows family structure and a within-family geometric indicator that point in the same direction as the behavioural contrast, without proving causation. The remainder of this Discussion explains that single answer through two lenses — the control-versus-shift contrast in §§V.B–V.E, and the representation-space evidence in §V.F — and converts it into protocol-bounded practical implications in §V.G.

### V.B. What pretraining helps with

Transfer learning in crystal-graph materials ML is conventionally defended as a two-part claim: pretrained representations improve target-task accuracy over random initialization on the same architecture and protocol, and they do so most strongly when target-family data are scarce [CITE: Lee & Asahi 2021; Kim et al. 2024]. Our combined evidence supports the first part on both arms and qualifies the second.

Both families show a pretrained-over-scratch gap that is large in absolute terms at the two scratch-tested sizes — gaps of 0.5038 and 0.2214 eV/atom on oxides and 0.5741 and 0.2706 eV/atom on nitrides at N = 50 and N = 500 (Results §III.C). The arithmetic ordering between families is not interpretable as a cross-family ranking of transfer efficiency: the nitride bars are inflated by weaker scratch anchors in absolute terms, and the nitride N = 50 bar measures pretrained-initialization advantage over scratch, not fine-tuning adaptation, because the corresponding fine-tuning row is itself the epoch-1 checkpoint under Set 1. The only clean cross-family adapted-vs-scratch cross-section under the canonical protocol is N = 500, where both families' fine-tuning rows show mean best epochs well above 1.0.

Two readings follow. First, the operational claim "pretraining beats random initialization on both families at the two scratch-tested sizes" is supported [CITE: Lee & Asahi 2021; Kim et al. 2024; Hu et al. 2024], and the arithmetic magnitude of that benefit depends on the scratch anchor's quality rather than on target-family adaptation quality. Second, the fine-tuning-vs-zero-shot gap is the more telling efficiency variable: on oxides it sits above zero-shot throughout the tested range and flattens toward it; on nitrides it sits above zero-shot and does not converge to it within the tested range. Pretraining helps both families relative to a no-pretraining counterfactual; fine-tuning's residual contribution on top of pretraining is small and positive on oxides and does not push nitride below its own zero-shot baseline at any tested size under the canonical protocol.

### V.C. What chemical-family mismatch costs under the canonical protocol

Literature on OOD generalization in materials ML reports, cautiously, that family- or composition-held-out splits produce larger errors than random splits, and that transfer-learning gains shrink as chemical distance between source and target grows [CITE: Omee et al. 2024; Li et al. 2025 — OOD; Li et al. 2025 — adversarial; Hu et al. 2024]. Our combined evidence concretizes this in the specific setting of a formation-energy ALIGNN pretrained on broadly JARVIS data and evaluated on a chemically distinct target family (nitrides), and it does so on three measurement surfaces.

The first cost is visible before any target-family training: the zero-shot family gap of 2.03× in test MAE (Results §III.A) is the cleanest quantitative signature of the in-distribution versus out-of-distribution split, because it removes every confound introduced by fine-tuning. The second cost is visible in the fine-tuning timing contrast (Results §III.B). Oxide fine-tuning enters genuine multi-epoch optimization at N = 50 (mean best epoch 18.5) and stabilizes by N = 200 (mean best epoch 39.0); nitride fine-tuning remains pinned at mean best epoch 1.0 from N = 10 through N = 200 and only engages at N = 500 (40.5) and N = 1000 (45.0). The asymmetry is in *when* multi-epoch optimization engages at all, a behaviour that final-MAE-only tables do not surface. The third cost is visible in the residual gap at adapted configurations (Results §III.D): at N = 500 and N = 1000, with both families in the adapted regime, oxide mean test MAE sits within 0.01 eV/atom of oxide zero-shot, while nitride sits 0.02–0.03 eV/atom above nitride zero-shot.

The combined reading, scoped to the canonical protocol, is that chemical-family mismatch on this target is not a transient low-data effect that more samples within the tested range would dissolve, and it is not a single-MAE artefact. It is a consistent penalty that appears at the pretrained starting point, governs whether the optimizer moves off that starting point under Set 1, and persists in the adapted regime within the tested N range. Whether a different optimization schedule would change the magnitude or character of any of these surfaces is not a question the present protocol can answer either way; the penalty we describe is the penalty observed under the canonical Set 1 protocol.

### V.D. Why oxide is easier

Oxides sit within the operating regime of a broadly JARVIS-trained representation by study design [CITE: Choudhary et al. 2020 — JARVIS; Choudhary & DeCost 2021 — ALIGNN]. Three empirical features of the oxide arm are consistent with this positioning without requiring any stronger claim about the chemical composition of the pretraining corpus.

The pretrained checkpoint already performs well zero-shot on the oxide test set (0.0342 eV/atom), the best observed oxide configuration under Set 1 in this study; this is the expected shape of transfer on chemistry-aligned targets [CITE: Choudhary et al. 2024 — JARVIS-Leaderboard]. The fine-tuning loop willingly leaves initialization at small N, with `mean_best_epoch` rising to 18.5 at N = 50 and plateauing at 35–39 from N = 200 onward, consistent with sustained multi-epoch optimization on oxide splits large enough to move the model. And the oxide arm is internally reproducible at scale, with cross-seed standard deviation tightening from 0.0111 at N = 10 to 0.0053 at N = 1000. Read as a control, the oxide arm's "easier" status is what makes the nitride arm interpretable: it is the regime in which the pretrained representation works as advertised under the canonical protocol, so deviations elsewhere can be measured against a concrete reference behaviour rather than against an idealized expectation.

### V.E. Why nitride is harder

Nitrides are the chemically distinct target family of the study. Three empirical features of the nitride arm are consistent with treating chemical distance as the operative source of difficulty under the canonical protocol [CITE: Omee et al. 2024; Li et al. 2025 — OOD; Hu et al. 2024].

The zero-shot MAE is elevated before any target-family training: 2.03× the oxide comparator on matched evaluation. Because the same checkpoint produces both numbers under identical protocol and differs only in the target family, the zero-shot gap isolates a family-level discrepancy at the pretrained starting point itself. The low-`N` inertness pattern is not a data-quantity artefact: the oxide comparator under the same Set 1 protocol and in the same low-`N` band has already entered genuine multi-epoch optimization, so if nitride inertness were only a noisy-gradient story it should appear on oxides too, and it does not. The candidate that survives this control is a domain-shift reading: the `mean_best_epoch = 1.0` signature at nitride N ≤ 200 reads as an optimizer that cannot find a nearby point better than the pretrained initialization within the canonical 50-epoch budget, not as a failure of small-data training in general. And the residual gap persists at adapted configurations: the best adapted nitride configuration (N = 1000) still sits above the nitride zero-shot baseline.

The compact nitride reading is therefore that the arm is harder not because nitrides are intrinsically harder materials to model, but because the pretrained formation-energy representation is operating outside the regime where it can cheaply adapt to small target-family budgets under the canonical protocol, and because the penalty associated with that fact persists under the tested N range and schedule.

### V.F. What the embedding analysis adds beyond performance curves

MAE tables, learning curves, and parity plots are outputs of the model. They describe the domain-shift penalty as a behavioural property — its size at zero-shot, when fine-tuning begins to engage, and whether adapted fine-tuning closes the family gap. They do not localize difficulty to anything in the representation the model is reading from. The embedding analysis answers a different question: does the frozen pretrained representation, examined before any target-family training, already show structure consistent with the behavioural penalty? [CITE: Omee et al. 2024; Li et al. 2025 — OOD; Li et al. 2025 — adversarial]

The answer is three-part (Results §IV). Family structure is already present in the frozen representation to the point where a linear probe recovers family labels from raw 256-D `last_alignn_pool` embeddings almost perfectly (logistic-regression family AUC 0.9994). The pretrained network has organized the test set along the oxide/nitride axis without supervision on that axis, bringing the pretrained representation — not only the downstream regression head — into view as a geometric correlate of the family contrast. The family structure is asymmetric in raw space: by per-family silhouette and per-family 15-NN purity, the oxide region is more internally cohesive than the nitride region, and that asymmetry aligns with the direction of the behavioural asymmetry. And the embedding analysis introduces a within-family geometric indicator of relative difficulty that aggregate MAE cannot resolve: 5-nearest-oxide distance co-varies with nitride zero-shot error across the full nitride test population (Spearman ρ = 0.3428, FDR q = 1.3 × 10⁻⁴), with the hard-versus-easy distance gap stable under alternative distance definitions reported in the appendix.

None of these additions replaces the behavioural evidence, and none demonstrates causality. What the embedding analysis adds is a representation-space counterpart to the behavioural pattern — a geometric correlate that makes the domain-shift interpretation legible without upgrading it to a mechanism claim. Shared upstream factors — bonding chemistry, coordination environment, local symmetry — may jointly drive both displacement from the oxide-reference region and the unreliability of the pretrained head on particular structures [CITE: Omee et al. 2024; Li et al. 2025 — adversarial]. PCA, t-SNE, and UMAP projections are descriptive visual support only; visual inter-cluster distances are not quoted as statistical evidence, because such projections preserve local structure but not global inter-cluster geometry in a physically interpretable way [CITE: van der Maaten & Hinton 2008; McInnes et al. 2018]. All quantitative claims in the embedding section are computed in the raw 256-D `last_alignn_pool` space.

### V.G. Practical implications for small-data materials discovery

Because the combined paper is a comparative manuscript, its practical implications are most usefully stated as two distinct workflow profiles: one for chemistry-aligned targets and one for chemistry-shifted targets. Each is scoped explicitly to the regime tested (formation energy per atom, JARVIS `dft_3d` splits, the pretrained formation-energy ALIGNN checkpoint, Hyperparameter Set 1, six N values up to 1,000) and to the two scratch-tested sizes.

**Chemistry-aligned targets (the oxide profile).** First, in this regime the choice of initialization is the dominant data-efficiency lever: at N = 500 the pretrained-vs-scratch gap is 0.2214 eV/atom on oxides, whereas the difference between the N = 500 and N = 1000 fine-tuning means is roughly 0.001 eV/atom. If a project budget must be spent on one decision, spending it on obtaining a strong pretrained starting point is a much better investment than spending it on additional labelled target-family data, within the tested N range. Second, pretrained zero-shot is a defensible first-pass estimator on chemistry-aligned formation-energy targets in a comparable regime: under Set 1 it is the best observed oxide configuration in this study, and fine-tuning is most useful for tightening cross-seed reproducibility (per-seed standard deviation roughly halves across the tested N range) rather than for producing the headline accuracy.

**Chemistry-shifted targets (the nitride profile).** First, on chemically distant targets in a comparable regime, a few hundred labelled target-family structures should not be counted on to beat the pretrained zero-shot baseline under a standard fine-tuning protocol. The tested range up to N = 1000 does not produce a nitride fine-tuning configuration below nitride zero-shot under Set 1; the threshold at which the optimizer engages at all sits between N = 200 and N = 500 under the canonical protocol. Project planning on comparable targets should adopt this as the default expectation rather than the exception. Second, on shifted targets the pretrained-vs-scratch gap — not the fine-tune-vs-zero-shot residual — is the more defensible measure of transfer value to communicate, because the residual is in this regime negative; the order-of-magnitude transfer claim (0.2706 eV/atom at N = 500 on nitrides) is the robust one.

**Spanning both profiles.** Pretrained-space geometry can serve as a within-family triage signal on shifted tasks: the moderate but significant correlation between 5-nearest-oxide distance and absolute zero-shot error (Spearman ρ = 0.3428, FDR q = 1.3 × 10⁻⁴) suggests a representation-based hardness score for allocating additional labelling effort or uncertainty estimation. We stop short of proposing this as a deployment-ready tool: the correlation is moderate, hard and easy distance distributions overlap substantially, and the association is reported for absolute zero-shot error only.

### V.H. Limitations

Six limitations bound the combined claim. (i) **Protocol scope.** All results are reported under Hyperparameter Set 1 (epochs = 50, learning rate = 1 × 10⁻⁴, batch size = 16). We do not claim that nitride fine-tuning is incapable of beating nitride zero-shot under alternative schedules; we claim only that it does not within the tested protocol and N range, and the nitride low-`N` inertness is documented under the canonical Set 1 protocol. (ii) **Scratch coverage.** From-scratch baselines exist only at N = 50 and N = 500 for both families; no continuous transfer-benefit curve is inferred, and under the nitride N = 50 initialization-advantage caveat the only clean cross-family adapted-vs-scratch cross-section is N = 500. (iii) **Fine-tuning range.** Six N values were tested up to N = 1000; we do not claim a demonstrated saturation on oxides or a demonstrated floor on nitrides at larger N. (iv) **Test-set sizes.** The oxide test set is 1,484 structures and the nitride test set is 242 structures; the sampling uncertainty on the two zero-shot MAE values is not identical, and the 2× family-gap statement is directional rather than tightly pinned. (v) **Checkpoint scope.** The checkpoint is the pretrained formation-energy ALIGNN model distributed through the JARVIS infrastructure; we do not characterize its training corpus chemically and deliberately do not describe it as "oxide-pretrained." "Oxide-reference region" is used only in the distance-context language of Results IV and §V.F. (vi) **Embedding causality.** Raw-space `last_alignn_pool` metrics and the distance–error association are descriptive and correlational; they do not quantify how much of the behavioural MAE gap is attributable to representation geometry, and they do not constitute mechanism proof.

### V.I. Future work

Four directions extend the combined analysis. Per-family hyperparameter tuning would quantify how much of the nitride low-`N` inertness is protocol-contingent under the canonical evaluation conditions; lower learning rates, longer budgets, or discriminative schedules could plausibly move `mean_best_epoch` off 1.0 at N ≤ 200. Scratch baselines at N ∈ {10, 100, 200, 1000} in both families would convert the present two-point pretrained-vs-scratch comparison into a continuous transfer-benefit curve and would allow the nitride N = 50 caveat to be benchmarked against neighbouring sizes. Extending the distance–error analysis from zero-shot error to fine-tuned error at N = 500 and N = 1000 would test whether the geometric correlate of difficulty survives adaptation or relaxes with it, and whether the within-family indicator generalizes to the adapted regime. A compositional, coordination-environment, or structural-prototype decomposition of the nitride residual at high `N` would test which subpopulations drive the parity dispersion and the tail of the distance–error distribution, and would begin to move the embedding view from a geometric correlate toward a chemistry-interpretable one.

## VI. Conclusion

This paper asked how a pretrained formation-energy ALIGNN model behaves on an in-distribution oxide control task versus an out-of-distribution nitride test task, and whether pretrained embedding-space geometry could help interpret the difference in adaptation difficulty.

The combined evidence supports a single control-versus-shift answer. On the in-distribution control task, the pretrained checkpoint is already strong, canonical fine-tuning converges toward but does not cross that benchmark, and pretrained initialization beats random initialization by a wide margin at both scratch-tested sizes. On the out-of-distribution test task under the canonical protocol, the same checkpoint incurs a 2× family-level zero-shot penalty, the canonical fine-tuning loop is operationally inert at N ≤ 200 — `mean_best_epoch = 1.0` across all seeds, so the selected checkpoint is effectively the pretrained zero-shot state — and the genuinely-adapted configurations at N = 500 and N = 1000 remain above the nitride zero-shot baseline. In the frozen `last_alignn_pool` representation from which both behaviours are computed, family labels are near-perfectly recoverable, the nitride region is less cohesive than the oxide region, and within nitrides, zero-shot error co-varies with distance from the oxide-reference region — a correlational geometric indicator consistent with a representation-space shift, not a causal mechanism proof.

The combined practical reading is that the choice of pretrained initialization is the dominant data-efficiency lever on both families in the regime tested, that zero-shot is a defensible first-pass estimator on chemistry-aligned targets and a non-negotiable baseline on chemically distant ones, and that pretrained-space geometry can serve as a modest within-family triage indicator on shifted tasks.

Read together, these findings position chemical-family domain shift not as a failure mode of transfer learning in materials ML but as a quantifiable, geometrically legible cost — one that, under the canonical protocol, separates the chemistry-aligned regime in which transfer works as advertised from the chemistry-shifted regime in which the labelled-data cost of moving below a pretrained baseline is substantially higher than chemistry-aligned task experience would predict, and that should therefore be planned for rather than discovered after the fact.
