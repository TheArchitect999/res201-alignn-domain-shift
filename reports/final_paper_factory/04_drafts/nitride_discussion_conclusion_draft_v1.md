## 5. Discussion

### 5.1 Reading the four-step arc as a domain-shift story

The Results act built the nitride evidence in four steps: (Step 1, §4.1) a zero-shot domain-shift penalty of roughly 2× relative to the oxide comparator; (Step 2, §4.2) operational inertness of fine-tuning at N ∈ {10, 50, 100, 200} under Hyperparameter Set 1, with `mean_best_epoch = 1.0` at every seed; (Step 3, §4.3) genuine but incomplete adaptation at N = 500 and N = 1000, where the optimizer traverses a substantial portion of the 50-epoch budget but mean test MAE does not recover the nitride zero-shot baseline; and (Step 4, §§4.4–4.5) a representation-level view in which the nitride region of frozen `last_alignn_pool` space is distinguishable but less cohesive than the oxide region, and within the nitride family zero-shot error co-varies with distance from the oxide-reference region. A supporting pretrained-vs-from-scratch comparison (§4.6) confirmed that pretraining remains operationally valuable on nitrides but did not revise the arc.

The Discussion reads those four steps as a single coherent finding: on the out-of-distribution family studied here, the domain-shift penalty is not a transient low-data artifact that larger labelled budgets dissolve, and it is not an optimization accident that a better learning-rate schedule would remove. It is present at the pretrained starting point, it governs whether the canonical fine-tuning loop will move off that starting point at all, and it persists in the error distribution of the genuinely-adapted models at N = 500 and N = 1000. The representation-level view reinforces the behavioural reading without upgrading the interpretation to a causal one: the pretrained space already encodes a family-level structure before any nitride fine-tuning is performed, and where a given nitride lies in that space tracks with how hard the pretrained head finds it to predict.

### 5.2 What pretraining helps with on nitrides — and where the help runs out

Transfer learning in materials informatics is commonly described as a two-part claim: pretrained representations are valuable over random initialization, and that value is most pronounced when target-family data are scarce [CITE: Lee & Asahi 2021; Kim et al. 2024]. Our nitride evidence confirms the first part and places a boundary on the second.

Pretraining beats random initialization on nitrides by a wide margin at both scratch-tested sample sizes. At N = 50, the from-scratch MAE is 0.6914 ± 0.0163 eV/atom against 0.1173 eV/atom on the fine-tuned side, a 0.5741 eV/atom gap; at N = 500 the from-scratch MAE is 0.3683 ± 0.0233 eV/atom against 0.0977 ± 0.0178, a 0.2706 eV/atom gap. Both from-scratch means sit well above the 0.0695 eV/atom zero-shot baseline, so random initialization on nitride alone cannot match what a broadly pretrained ALIGNN gives at either tested scale. This is the standard-form transfer-learning result [CITE: Lee & Asahi 2021; Hu et al. 2024] and it is real on nitrides.

But the two gaps are asymmetric in meaning and must not be averaged into a single claim. The N = 50 gap measures pretrained-initialization advantage over scratch, not fine-tuning adaptation, because the corresponding fine-tuning row has `mean_best_epoch = 1.0` and is operationally still the pretrained zero-shot checkpoint with a single optimizer step applied. The N = 500 gap is the only clean adapted-vs-scratch comparison we have; its fine-tuning row has `mean_best_epoch = 40.5`. A one-line reading of both rows together is: on nitrides, the step that delivers value is the step of starting from a pretrained representation in the first place; the additional step of fine-tuning that representation on more labelled nitride data reduces error further at N = 500, but does so from a still-higher-than-zero-shot baseline.

The boundary the nitride evidence draws is operational, not theoretical. Under the canonical protocol (epochs = 50, learning rate = 1 × 10⁻⁴, batch size = 16, five seeds per configuration) and within the six N values tested up to N = 1,000, no budget produces a fine-tuned model with mean test MAE below the nitride zero-shot baseline. We do not claim that no protocol can ever do so; we do claim that, on an out-of-distribution family, the standard expectation that a few hundred labelled target-family structures will beat the pretrained checkpoint is not met here.

### 5.3 Why nitride is harder: not only a data-quantity problem

Two candidate explanations for poor low-`N` nitride performance must be ruled in or out. The first is the ordinary small-data story: with only 10–200 labelled structures, gradient estimates are too noisy to move the model into a region better than its initialization. The second is a domain-shift story: the pretrained initialization sits in a region of parameter space that, for the nitride distribution, is not a good basin to descend from in the first place, so the canonical optimizer cannot leave the initialization even as more labelled nitride data arrive, up to the point where the budget is large enough to force a meaningful update.

The oxide control discriminates between these explanations. Under the same Set 1 protocol and within roughly the same low-`N` band (N = 50, 100, 200), oxide fine-tuning has already entered genuine multi-epoch optimization: oxide `mean_best_epoch` rises from 18.5 at N = 50 to 39.0 at N = 200 and 35.5 at N = 1000. If the low-`N` nitride inertness were only a data-quantity effect it should appear on oxide too, and it does not. The asymmetry is in when multi-epoch optimization engages at all, not in final MAE. This is consistent with a domain-shift reading in which the pretrained formation-energy representation behaves, on nitrides, as if it is already close to the best reachable point within the early-stopping budget on small nitride splits — and is therefore reluctant to move.

Literature on OOD generalization in materials ML supports this interpretation in its cautious form: family- or composition-held-out splits produce larger errors than random splits, and transfer-learning gains shrink as chemical distance between source and target grows [CITE: Omee et al. 2024; Li et al. 2025 — OOD; Hu et al. 2024]. Our result is compatible with that picture and adds a sharper operational claim: on a chemically distinct target family, the canonical protocol's failure mode at low `N` is not a noisy small-data gradient but an optimizer that stays pinned at the pretrained checkpoint — the `mean_best_epoch = 1.0` signature is not a failure to converge, it is convergence to initialization.

### 5.4 What the embedding analysis adds beyond the performance curves

MAE tables, learning curves, and parity plots describe the domain-shift penalty as a behavioural property of the model — how large it is, when it appears, and whether fine-tuning budget reduces it. They do not localize relative difficulty to anything in the representation the model is reading from. The embedding analysis answers a different question: does the frozen pretrained representation, examined before any nitride fine-tuning, show structure consistent with the behavioural penalty?

The answer we report is three-part. First, family structure is already present in the frozen representation: a linear probe recovers family labels from the raw 256-D `last_alignn_pool` embeddings almost perfectly (logistic-regression family AUC 0.9994), and 15-nearest-neighbour family purity is 0.9655 overall. The pretrained network has built a representation in which oxides and nitrides occupy distinguishable regions without being supervised on that distinction. Second, the two family regions are not symmetric in raw space: by per-family silhouette (oxide 0.2546, nitride 0.1453) and by per-family 15-NN purity (oxide 0.9872, nitride 0.8331), the nitride region is distinguishable from oxides but internally less cohesive than the oxide region. Third, within the nitride family, zero-shot error co-varies with distance from the oxide-reference region in frozen pretrained space: the 49 hardest nitrides (top 20% by absolute zero-shot error) have a mean 5-nearest-oxide distance of 4.5988 against 3.7821 for the 49 easiest, a hard-minus-easy gap of 0.8168 (95% CI [0.4746, 1.1597], FDR q = 1.8 × 10⁻⁴), and a full-sample Spearman correlation of 0.3428 (95% CI [0.2214, 0.4597], FDR q = 1.3 × 10⁻⁴).

This is the distinctive content of the embedding analysis relative to the performance section. The behavioural curves establish *that* the domain-shift penalty is present; the embedding analysis shows that the pretrained representation carries a family-level geometric signature that is aligned with the direction of the penalty, and that within-family variation in that geometry co-varies with within-family variation in zero-shot error. The embedding view therefore does two things the performance view cannot: it gives the family-level penalty a representation-space counterpart, and it introduces a within-family geometric indicator of relative difficulty that aggregate MAE cannot resolve.

We frame these findings as consistent with a representation-space shift, not as causal proof of one, and we are deliberate about this wording. The logistic-regression AUC is a family-recoverability statistic, not a mechanism statistic: near-perfect family separation in raw space is compatible with many downstream error patterns. The distance–error association is correlational; shared upstream factors — bonding chemistry, coordination environment, local symmetry — may plausibly drive both a structure's displacement from the oxide-reference region and the pretrained regression head's unreliability on it. Cautious correlational framing of representation-based OOD diagnostics is standard in materials ML [CITE: Omee et al. 2024; Li et al. 2025 — OOD; Li et al. 2025 — adversarial]. Two-dimensional projections (PCA, t-SNE, UMAP) are descriptive visual support only; visual inter-cluster distances are not quoted as statistical evidence because such projections preserve local structure but not global geometry in a physically interpretable way [CITE: van der Maaten & Hinton 2008; McInnes et al. 2018].

### 5.5 Practical implications for small-data materials discovery

Three practical implications follow from the nitride evidence, each stated cautiously because each is a claim about a specific out-of-distribution setting rather than a general prescription.

First, on chemically distant target families, a few hundred labelled target-family structures should not be counted on to beat a pretrained zero-shot baseline under a standard fine-tuning protocol. Our tested range up to N = 1000 does not produce such a configuration on nitrides; under Set 1 the practical rule is that the labelled-data budget needed to move off the pretrained initialization at all is substantially larger than the low-`N` end of a typical materials workflow. The specific threshold where the optimizer engages (somewhere between N = 200 and N = 500 under the canonical protocol) is a useful reference point for project planning on comparable targets, though we do not claim it transfers to other families without testing.

Second, even when target-family data are sufficient to adapt, the pretrained-vs-scratch gap is the more defensible transfer-value number than the fine-tuning-vs-zero-shot gap. On nitrides at N = 500, the pretrained route sits 0.2706 eV/atom below scratch while remaining 0.0281 eV/atom above zero-shot; the "pretraining helps" claim is the order-of-magnitude one, not the residual adaptation improvement. A workflow decision that trusts the scratch baseline to calibrate the value of pretraining is more robust than one that relies on fine-tuning's incremental improvement over zero-shot, which in our nitride regime is negative.

Third, the distance–error correlate suggests that pretrained-space geometry can be used as a within-family triage signal on chemically distant tasks. Structures whose pretrained embeddings sit far from a reference region (here, the oxide train+val pool as the heavier-represented family in the training data) tend to be those on which the pretrained head is least reliable zero-shot. This is a modest operational suggestion — a representation-based hardness score for budgeting where to allocate additional labelling effort or uncertainty estimation — and it is consistent with emerging OOD-diagnosis practice in materials ML [CITE: Omee et al. 2024; Li et al. 2025 — OOD]. We stop short of proposing it as a deployment-ready tool; the correlation is moderate (Spearman ρ = 0.3428), hard and easy distance distributions overlap substantially, and the association is reported for absolute zero-shot error only.

### 5.6 Limitations

Five limitations bound the nitride claim. (i) **Protocol scope.** All results are reported under Hyperparameter Set 1. A different learning-rate schedule, a longer training budget, or a more aggressive early-layer unfreezing policy might push nitride fine-tuning into genuine adaptation at lower `N` or push the N = 1000 configuration below zero-shot. We do not claim nitride fine-tuning is incapable of beating zero-shot, only that it does not within the tested protocol and `N` range. (ii) **Scratch coverage.** From-scratch nitride baselines exist only at N = 50 and N = 500; no continuous transfer-benefit curve is inferred, and under the N = 50 initialization-advantage caveat the only clean adapted-vs-scratch cross-section is N = 500. (iii) **Sample sizes.** The nitride test set is 242 structures; sampling uncertainty on the nitride zero-shot MAE is larger than on the 1,484-structure oxide comparator, so the 2× family gap statement is directional but not tightly pinned. (iv) **Checkpoint scope.** The pretrained checkpoint is the formation-energy ALIGNN model distributed through the JARVIS infrastructure. We do not characterize its training corpus chemically and deliberately do not describe it as "oxide-pretrained"; the composition of that corpus is consistent with a pretraining regime more aligned with oxides than nitrides, and "oxide-reference region" is used only in distance-context language in §§4.5 and 5.4. (v) **Embedding causality.** The distance–error association is correlational. It is consistent with a representation-space shift and is reported as a geometric indicator; it is not mechanistic proof, and shared upstream factors may drive both geometry and error.

### 5.7 Future work

Four directions extend the nitride analysis without revising its conclusion. First, per-family hyperparameter tuning would quantify how much of the low-`N` inertness is protocol-contingent; a lower learning rate combined with a longer budget, or a discriminative fine-tuning schedule, could plausibly move `mean_best_epoch` off 1.0 at N ≤ 200 and would sharpen the split between "domain shift" and "canonical-protocol stall." Second, scratch baselines at N ∈ {10, 100, 200, 1000} would turn the two-point pretrained-vs-scratch comparison into a continuous transfer-benefit curve and allow the initialization-advantage caveat at N = 50 to be benchmarked against neighbouring sizes. Third, a compositional, coordination-environment, or structural-prototype decomposition of the nitride residual would test which subpopulations drive the high-`N` parity dispersion and the tail of the distance–error distribution. Fourth, extending the distance–error analysis from zero-shot error to fine-tuned error at N = 500 and N = 1000 would ask whether the geometric correlate of difficulty survives adaptation or relaxes with it; that result is not in scope here.

## 6. Conclusion

This report asked how a pretrained formation-energy ALIGNN model behaves when the target family lies outside the chemistry most aligned with the pretraining distribution, and whether fine-tuning on labelled target-family data can overcome the resulting penalty under the canonical protocol.

The evidence supports a single domain-shift answer with four steps. The pretrained model starts at a nitride zero-shot MAE of 0.0695 eV/atom, roughly 2× the oxide comparator's 0.0342 eV/atom, before any target-family training. Under Hyperparameter Set 1, nitride fine-tuning at N ∈ {10, 50, 100, 200} is operationally inert — `mean_best_epoch = 1.0` at every seed across all four sizes — and the selected checkpoint at each of those budgets is effectively the pretrained zero-shot state with a single optimizer step applied. At N = 500 and N = 1000 the optimizer engages (mean best epochs 40.5 and 45.0) and cross-seed variability tightens, but the best genuinely-adapted configuration (N = 1000, mean test MAE 0.0907 eV/atom) still sits 0.0211 eV/atom above the nitride zero-shot baseline. In the frozen `last_alignn_pool` representation, the nitride region is distinguishable but less cohesive than the oxide region (nitride silhouette 0.1453 vs oxide 0.2546; nitride 15-NN purity 0.8331 vs oxide 0.9872), and within the nitride family zero-shot error co-varies with distance from the oxide-reference region (Spearman ρ = 0.3428, FDR q = 1.3 × 10⁻⁴; hard-minus-easy distance gap 0.8168, FDR q = 1.8 × 10⁻⁴) — a correlational geometric indicator consistent with a representation-space shift, not a causal mechanism proof.

The practical implication for small-data materials discovery on chemically distant targets is conservative: pretrained initialization is valuable on nitrides in the order-of-magnitude sense that it beats random initialization at every tested size, but in the nitride regime studied here fine-tuning under the canonical protocol does not close the family gap at the budgets materials workflows typically operate at. Labelled-data planning on such targets should assume that the labelled-data cost of moving below the pretrained zero-shot baseline is substantially higher than would be expected from a chemistry-aligned task, and that pretrained-space geometry can be used as a within-family indicator of where that cost will be hardest to pay.
