## V. Discussion

### V.A. One finding, four consistent faces

Results I–IV developed a single comparative finding, measured four different ways. Results I (oxide) showed a strong zero-shot starting point (0.0342 eV/atom MAE on 1,484 oxide test structures) that canonical fine-tuning converges toward but does not cross, with pretrained initialization dominating random initialization by 0.5038 eV/atom at N = 50 and 0.2214 eV/atom at N = 500. Results II (nitride) showed a zero-shot starting point roughly 2× higher (0.0695 eV/atom on 242 test structures), operational inertness of fine-tuning at N ≤ 200 under Hyperparameter Set 1 (`mean_best_epoch = 1.0` across all seeds at every one of those data sizes), and genuine but incomplete adaptation at N = 500 (mean best epoch 40.5, mean test MAE 0.0977 eV/atom) and N = 1000 (mean best epoch 45.0, mean test MAE 0.0907 eV/atom). Results III (direct comparison) placed those two trajectories on the same axes and quantified the family contrast: a 2.03× zero-shot family gap, a cross-family asymmetry in when multi-epoch optimization begins (oxide by N = 50, nitride only at N = 500), and a transfer-benefit contrast at the two scratch-tested sizes (N = 50 and N = 500) with the nitride N = 50 bar reading as pretrained-initialization advantage over scratch, not fine-tuning adaptation, because that nitride fine-tuning row is itself the epoch-1 checkpoint. Results IV (embedding analysis) described the pretrained representation from which those outputs are computed: raw 256-D `last_alignn_pool` geometry in which family labels are near-perfectly recoverable (logistic-regression family AUC 0.9994), the nitride region is less cohesive than the oxide region (nitride silhouette 0.1453 vs oxide 0.2546; nitride 15-NN purity 0.8331 vs oxide 0.9872), and, within the nitride test set, zero-shot error co-varies with distance from the oxide-reference region (Spearman ρ = 0.3428, FDR q = 1.3 × 10⁻⁴; hard-minus-easy distance gap 0.8168, FDR q = 1.8 × 10⁻⁴).

The discussion that follows reads these four faces as one domain-shift story, keeps literature context separate from our findings, and answers six questions the combined Results block raises: what pretraining helps with, what chemical-family mismatch costs, why the oxide arm is easier, why the nitride arm is harder, what the embedding analysis adds beyond performance curves, and what all of this implies for small-data materials discovery.

### V.B. What pretraining helps with

Transfer learning in crystal-graph materials ML is conventionally defended as a two-part claim: pretrained representations improve target-task accuracy over random initialization on the same architecture and protocol, and they do so most strongly when target-family data are scarce [CITE: Lee & Asahi 2021; Kim et al. 2024]. Our combined evidence supports the first part in both arms and qualifies the second.

Both families show a pretrained-over-scratch gap that is large in absolute terms at both tested scratch sizes. For oxides, the gap is 0.5038 eV/atom at N = 50 and 0.2214 eV/atom at N = 500, shrinking with sample size because the from-scratch learning curve improves faster than the fine-tuned learning curve (scratch 0.5561 → 0.2643 eV/atom, fine-tune 0.0523 → 0.0430 eV/atom) as fine-tune is already bounded near the zero-shot benchmark. For nitrides, the gap is 0.5741 eV/atom at N = 50 and 0.2706 eV/atom at N = 500, but the arithmetic is not interpretable as a cross-family ranking of transfer efficiency because the nitride bars are inflated by a weaker scratch anchor at both sizes and because the nitride N = 50 value reflects pretrained-initialization advantage over scratch, not fine-tuning adaptation (the corresponding fine-tuning row has `mean_best_epoch = 1.0` under Set 1). The only clean cross-family adapted-vs-scratch comparison we have is at N = 500, where both families' fine-tuning rows show mean best epochs well above 1.0.

Taken together, the transfer-benefit comparison establishes the operational claim "pretraining beats random initialization on both families at the two scratch-tested sizes" [CITE: Lee & Asahi 2021; Kim et al. 2024; Hu et al. 2024], and it establishes that the arithmetic magnitude of that benefit depends on the scratch anchor's quality rather than on the quality of target-family adaptation. The fine-tuning-vs-zero-shot gap is the more telling variable for reading efficiency. On oxides the fine-tuning curve sits above zero-shot throughout the tested range and flattens toward it; on nitrides the fine-tuning curve sits above zero-shot and does not converge to it within the tested range. Pretraining helps both families relative to a no-pretraining counterfactual; fine-tuning's residual contribution on top of pretraining is small and positive on oxides, and does not push the nitride configuration below its own zero-shot baseline at any tested size.

### V.C. What chemical-family mismatch costs

Literature on OOD generalization in materials ML reports, cautiously, that family- or composition-held-out splits produce larger errors than random splits, and that transfer-learning gains shrink as chemical distance between source and target grows [CITE: Omee et al. 2024; Li et al. 2025 — OOD; Li et al. 2025 — adversarial; Hu et al. 2024]. Our combined evidence concretizes this in the specific setting of a formation-energy ALIGNN pretrained on broadly JARVIS data and evaluated on a chemically distinct target family (nitrides), and it does so on three measurement surfaces rather than only on final MAE.

The first cost of chemical-family mismatch is visible before any target-family training: the zero-shot family gap of 2.03× in test MAE is the cleanest quantitative signature of the in-distribution versus out-of-distribution split, because it removes every confound introduced by fine-tuning (checkpoint selection, early stopping, seed variance, data-budget interaction). The second cost is visible in the fine-tuning timing contrast. Oxide fine-tuning enters genuine multi-epoch optimization at N = 50 (mean best epoch 18.5) and stabilizes by N = 200 (mean best epoch 39.0); nitride fine-tuning remains pinned at mean best epoch 1.0 from N = 10 through N = 200 and only engages at N = 500 (40.5) and N = 1000 (45.0). The asymmetry is in *when* multi-epoch optimization engages at all, which is a behaviour that final-MAE-only tables do not surface. The third cost is visible in the residual gap at adapted configurations: at N = 500 and N = 1000, where both families are in the adapted regime under Set 1, oxide mean test MAE sits 0.0088 and 0.0075 eV/atom above oxide zero-shot respectively, while nitride sits 0.0281 and 0.0211 eV/atom above nitride zero-shot; the family gap persists even when matched on hyperparameters and on adaptation status.

The combined reading is: on the out-of-distribution family studied here, chemical-family mismatch is not a transient low-data effect, nor an accident of random initialization that more seeds would smooth, nor a feature of one single MAE number. It is a consistent penalty that appears at the pretrained starting point, governs whether the optimizer moves off that starting point, and persists in the adapted regime.

### V.D. Why oxide is easier

Oxides sit within the operating regime of a broadly JARVIS-trained representation by study design [CITE: Choudhary et al. 2020 — JARVIS; Choudhary & DeCost 2021 — ALIGNN]. Three empirical features of the oxide arm are consistent with this positioning without requiring any stronger claim about the pretraining corpus.

First, the pretrained checkpoint already performs well zero-shot on the oxide test set (0.0342 eV/atom on 1,484 structures), which is the best observed oxide performance under Set 1 in this study. This is the expected shape of transfer on chemistry-aligned targets: zero-shot alone is strong because the pretrained representation is already deployed in a regime it understands [CITE: Choudhary et al. 2024 — JARVIS-Leaderboard]. Second, the fine-tuning loop willingly leaves initialization at small N: oxide `mean_best_epoch` is 18.5 at N = 50 and plateaus at 35–39 from N = 200 onward, consistent with sustained multi-epoch optimization on oxide splits that are large enough to move the model. Third, the oxide arm is internally reproducible at scale: cross-seed standard deviation narrows from 0.0111 at N = 10 to 0.0053 at N = 1000, and the high-`N` parity view is visually tight across the population (on-figure MAE 0.0383 at N = 1000). Under the canonical protocol, the oxide arm looks like the scenario the transfer-learning literature reports on chemistry-aligned tasks: a strong pretrained starting point, smooth adaptation, and shrinking variance with data.

Read as a control, the oxide arm's "easier" status is what makes it useful: it is the setting in which the pretrained representation is known to work, so every deviation elsewhere in the combined paper can be measured against a concrete reference behaviour rather than against an idealized expectation.

### V.E. Why nitride is harder

Nitrides are the chemically distinct target family of the study, with a less electronegative anion, higher covalency, and different coordination preferences than oxides. Three empirical features of the nitride arm are consistent with treating this chemical distance as the operative source of difficulty under the canonical protocol [CITE: Omee et al. 2024; Li et al. 2025 — OOD; Hu et al. 2024].

First, the zero-shot MAE is elevated before any target-family training occurs: 2.03× the oxide comparator on matched evaluation. Because this value is produced by the same pretrained checkpoint under identical protocol and differs only in the target family, the zero-shot gap isolates a family-level discrepancy at the pretrained starting point itself. Second, the low-`N` inertness pattern is not a data-quantity artifact. The oxide comparator, under the same Set 1 protocol and in the same low-`N` band (N = 50, 100, 200), has already entered genuine multi-epoch optimization; if the nitride inertness were only a matter of noisy gradients on small budgets it should appear on oxides too, and it does not. This narrows the candidate explanations: the `mean_best_epoch = 1.0` signature at nitride N ≤ 200 reads as an optimizer that cannot find a nearby point better than the pretrained initialization within the 50-epoch budget, not as a failure of small-data training in general. Third, the residual gap at adapted configurations shows that even when the optimizer engages at N ≥ 500 and cross-seed variability tightens, the family gap persists: the best adapted nitride configuration (N = 1000, mean test MAE 0.0907 eV/atom) still sits above the nitride zero-shot baseline.

The compact nitride reading is therefore: harder, not because nitrides are intrinsically harder materials to model, but because the pretrained formation-energy representation is operating outside the regime where it can cheaply adapt to small target-family budgets, and because the penalty associated with that fact is robust to increasing the labelled budget up to N = 1000 under Set 1.

### V.F. What the embedding analysis adds beyond performance curves

MAE tables, learning curves, and parity plots are outputs of the model. They describe the domain-shift penalty as a behavioural property — how large it is at zero-shot, when fine-tuning begins to engage, and whether adapted fine-tuning closes the family gap. They do not localize difficulty to anything in the representation the model is reading from. The embedding analysis asks a different question: does the frozen pretrained representation, examined before any target-family training, already show structure consistent with the behavioural penalty? [CITE: Omee et al. 2024; Li et al. 2025 — OOD; Li et al. 2025 — adversarial]

The answer we report contributes three pieces of information that the behavioural section cannot. First, family structure is already present in the frozen representation to the point where a linear probe recovers family labels from raw 256-D `last_alignn_pool` embeddings almost perfectly (logistic-regression family AUC 0.9994; overall 15-NN purity 0.9655). The pretrained network has organized the test set along the oxide/nitride axis without being supervised on that axis, and the pretrained representation — not only the downstream regression head — is therefore brought into view as a geometric correlate of the family contrast. Second, the family structure is asymmetric in raw space: by per-family silhouette and per-family 15-NN purity, the oxide region is more internally cohesive than the nitride region, and that asymmetry aligns with the direction of the behavioural asymmetry. Third, the embedding analysis introduces a within-family geometric indicator of relative difficulty that aggregate MAE cannot resolve: 5-nearest-oxide distance co-varies with zero-shot error across the full nitride test population (Spearman ρ = 0.3428, FDR q = 1.3 × 10⁻⁴), and the hard-minus-easy distance gap between the top and bottom 20% of nitrides by absolute zero-shot error is 0.8168 (FDR q = 1.8 × 10⁻⁴), stable under alternative distance definitions (centroid, Mahalanobis; appendix).

None of these additions replaces the behavioural evidence, and none demonstrates causality. What the embedding analysis adds is a representation-space counterpart to the behavioural pattern, which makes the domain-shift interpretation geometrically legible without upgrading it to a mechanism claim. Shared upstream factors — bonding chemistry, coordination environment, local symmetry — may jointly drive both geometric displacement from the oxide-reference region and the unreliability of the pretrained formation-energy head on particular structures [CITE: Omee et al. 2024; Li et al. 2025 — adversarial]. Two-dimensional projections (PCA, t-SNE, UMAP) are descriptive visual support only; visual inter-cluster distances are not quoted as statistical evidence, because such projections preserve local structure but not global inter-cluster geometry in a physically interpretable way [CITE: van der Maaten & Hinton 2008; McInnes et al. 2018]. All quantitative claims in the embedding section are computed in the raw 256-D `last_alignn_pool` space.

### V.G. Practical implications for small-data materials discovery

Five implications follow from the combined evidence, each stated cautiously because each is a claim about a specific transfer-learning setting (formation energy per atom, JARVIS `dft_3d` splits, Hyperparameter Set 1, six N values up to 1000) rather than a general prescription.

1. **The choice of initialization dominates the choice of fine-tuning budget.** At N = 500 under Set 1, the pretrained-vs-scratch gap is 0.2214 eV/atom on oxides and 0.2706 eV/atom on nitrides, while the difference between the N = 500 and N = 1000 fine-tuning mean MAEs is 0.0013 eV/atom on oxides and 0.0070 eV/atom on nitrides. If a project budget must be spent on one decision, spending it on obtaining a good pretrained starting point is a much better investment than spending it on additional labelled target-family data, up to roughly the sample sizes tested here.

2. **Pretrained zero-shot is a defensible first-pass estimator on chemistry-aligned targets.** On oxides, zero-shot is the best observed Set 1 oxide performance and fine-tuning does not cross it; for chemistry-aligned applications in a comparable regime, pretrained zero-shot can be used directly, with fine-tuning used to tighten cross-seed reproducibility (0.0111 → 0.0053 eV/atom std) rather than to produce the headline accuracy.

3. **On chemically distant targets, do not count on a few hundred labelled structures to beat the pretrained baseline under a standard fine-tuning protocol.** Our tested range up to N = 1000 does not produce a nitride fine-tuning configuration below nitride zero-shot under Set 1; the labelled-data cost of moving off the pretrained initialization is substantially higher on nitrides than on oxides, and the threshold where the optimizer engages at all is somewhere between N = 200 and N = 500 under the canonical protocol. Project planning on comparable targets should assume this regime rather than the chemistry-aligned regime as its default.

4. **Use the pretrained-vs-scratch gap rather than the fine-tune-vs-zero-shot gap to communicate transfer value on shifted targets.** On nitrides, the "pretraining helps" claim is the order-of-magnitude one (0.2706 eV/atom at N = 500), not the residual adaptation improvement (which is negative relative to zero-shot). The scratch comparison — bounded here to N = 50 and N = 500 because those are the only sizes at which we measured it — is the more robust way to defend transfer value on an out-of-distribution family.

5. **Pretrained-space geometry can be used as a within-family triage signal on shifted tasks.** The moderate but significant correlation between 5-nearest-oxide distance and absolute zero-shot error (Spearman ρ = 0.3428, FDR q = 1.3 × 10⁻⁴) suggests a representation-based hardness score for allocating additional labelling effort or uncertainty estimation. We stop short of proposing this as a deployment-ready tool; hard and easy distance distributions overlap substantially, the correlation is moderate, and the association is reported for absolute zero-shot error only.

### V.H. Limitations

Six limitations bound the combined claim. (i) **Protocol scope.** All results are reported under Hyperparameter Set 1 (epochs = 50, learning rate = 1 × 10⁻⁴, batch size = 16). We do not claim nitride fine-tuning is incapable of beating nitride zero-shot under alternative schedules, only that it does not do so within the tested protocol and `N` range. (ii) **Scratch coverage.** From-scratch baselines exist only at N = 50 and N = 500 for both families; no continuous transfer-benefit curve is inferred, and under the nitride N = 50 initialization-advantage caveat the only clean cross-family adapted-vs-scratch cross-section is N = 500. (iii) **Fine-tuning range.** Six N values were tested up to N = 1000; we do not claim a demonstrated saturation on oxides or a demonstrated floor on nitrides at larger N. (iv) **Test-set sizes.** The oxide test set is 1,484 structures and the nitride test set is 242 structures; the sampling uncertainty on the two zero-shot MAE values is therefore not identical, and the 2× family gap statement is directional rather than tightly pinned. (v) **Checkpoint scope.** The checkpoint is the pretrained formation-energy ALIGNN model distributed through the JARVIS infrastructure; we do not characterize its training corpus chemically and deliberately do not describe it as "oxide-pretrained." "Oxide-reference region" is used only in the distance-context language of Results IV and §V.F. (vi) **Embedding causality.** Raw-space `last_alignn_pool` metrics and the distance–error association are descriptive and correlational; they do not quantify how much of the behavioural MAE gap is attributable to representation geometry, and they do not constitute mechanism proof.

### V.I. Future work

Four directions extend the combined analysis. First, per-family hyperparameter tuning would quantify how much of the nitride low-`N` inertness is protocol-contingent; a lower learning rate combined with a longer budget, or a discriminative fine-tuning schedule, could plausibly move `mean_best_epoch` off 1.0 at N ≤ 200. Second, scratch baselines at N ∈ {10, 100, 200, 1000} in both families would turn the present two-point pretrained-vs-scratch comparison into a continuous transfer-benefit curve and would allow the nitride N = 50 caveat to be benchmarked against neighbouring sizes. Third, extending the distance–error analysis from zero-shot error to fine-tuned error at N = 500 and N = 1000 would ask whether the geometric correlate of difficulty survives adaptation or relaxes with it, and would test whether the within-family indicator generalizes to the adapted regime. Fourth, a compositional, coordination-environment, or structural-prototype decomposition of the nitride residual at high `N` would test which subpopulations drive the parity dispersion and the tail of the distance–error distribution, and would begin to move the embedding view from a geometric correlate toward a chemistry-interpretable one.

## VI. Conclusion

This paper asked how a pretrained formation-energy ALIGNN model behaves on an in-distribution oxide control task versus an out-of-distribution nitride test task, and whether pretrained embedding-space geometry could help explain the difference in adaptation difficulty.

The combined evidence supports a single control-versus-shift conclusion. On the in-distribution control task, the pretrained checkpoint is already strong (oxide zero-shot MAE 0.0342 eV/atom on 1,484 structures), canonical fine-tuning converges toward but does not cross that benchmark, and pretrained initialization beats random initialization by a wide margin at both tested scratch sizes (0.5038 eV/atom at N = 50; 0.2214 eV/atom at N = 500). On the out-of-distribution test task, the pretrained checkpoint incurs a 2× family-level penalty zero-shot (nitride MAE 0.0695 eV/atom on 242 structures), the canonical fine-tuning loop is operationally inert at N ≤ 200 — `mean_best_epoch = 1.0` across all seeds, so the selected checkpoint is effectively the pretrained zero-shot state — and the genuinely-adapted configurations at N = 500 and N = 1000 remain above the nitride zero-shot baseline. The transfer-benefit contrast at N = 500 (pretrained-over-scratch gaps of 0.2214 eV/atom on oxides and 0.2706 eV/atom on nitrides) confirms that pretraining retains operational value on both families, while the nitride N = 50 bar reads as pretrained-initialization advantage over scratch rather than as fine-tuning adaptation. In the frozen `last_alignn_pool` representation from which those behaviours are computed, family labels are near-perfectly recoverable (logistic-regression AUC 0.9994), the nitride region is less cohesive than the oxide region (silhouettes 0.1453 vs 0.2546; 15-NN purities 0.8331 vs 0.9872), and, within nitrides, zero-shot error co-varies with distance from the oxide-reference region (Spearman ρ = 0.3428, FDR q = 1.3 × 10⁻⁴) — a correlational geometric indicator consistent with a representation-space shift, not a causal mechanism proof.

The combined implication for small-data materials discovery is conservative and operational. The choice of pretrained initialization is the dominant data-efficiency lever on both families; zero-shot is a defensible first-pass estimator on chemistry-aligned targets and a non-negotiable baseline on chemically distant ones; transfer value on out-of-distribution families is most robustly defended by the pretrained-vs-scratch gap rather than by residual adaptation over zero-shot; and pretrained-space geometry can serve as a modest within-family indicator of where labelled-data investment is most needed. These findings position chemical-family domain shift not as a failure mode of transfer learning in materials ML but as a quantifiable, geometrically legible cost that — properly measured across zero-shot, learning curves, scratch baselines, and embedding structure — can be planned for rather than discovered after the fact.
