# Phase 10 — Discussion and Conclusion Revision Notes (v1 → v2)

This file accompanies the v2 rewrites of the three Discussion and Conclusion drafts produced in Phase 10. It records the largest cross-cutting changes, the largest manuscript-specific changes for each of the three documents, and any remaining ambiguities that need human sign-off before Phase 11 (Introduction, Abstract, and titles) and Phase 12 (assembly).

The v1 drafts were already evidence-correct and well-anchored to the canonical numbers. The v2 rewrites preserve every measured value and every citation placeholder, and do not introduce any new scientific claims. The revisions are entirely concentrated on rhetorical maturity, claim-bounding, interpretive sharpness, comparison structure, and conclusion elegance.

---

## 1. Largest cross-cutting changes (applied uniformly across all three documents)

The first cross-cutting change concerns counterfactual certainty about optimization schedules. In v1, several passages — particularly in the nitride manuscript and in §V.C of the combined paper — implied that the observed nitride penalty could not be removed by a better learning-rate schedule. Wording of that kind has been replaced throughout v2 with protocol-bounded phrasing: "under the canonical protocol," "within the tested N range and schedule," "is not resolved by the schedule tested here," and "persists under the canonical Set 1 protocol." The v2 drafts no longer state or imply that alternative optimization schedules are excluded in principle. They state only what the tested protocol actually demonstrates.

The second cross-cutting change rewrites the opening paragraph of every Discussion section. In v1, the openings were close to Results summaries — long, number-heavy, and structured to recapitulate evidence rather than to argue from it. The v2 openings each lead with the interpretive answer the Discussion will defend, identify the question being resolved, and use only the minimum result recap needed to set up the argument. This is the largest single change in feel between v1 and v2: the Discussion sections now begin by arguing rather than by re-listing.

The third cross-cutting change makes every practical implication explicitly protocol-bounded. In v1, several practical-implication lines — particularly in the oxide manuscript's §4.5 and the combined paper's §V.G — were broader in scope than the tested regime justifies. In v2, every practical recommendation is now anchored to the four-part scope the tested protocol actually covers: formation energy per atom, JARVIS `dft_3d` splits, the pretrained formation-energy ALIGNN checkpoint, and Hyperparameter Set 1 within the tested N range. The intent is not to weaken the implications but to make them reviewer-safe.

The fourth cross-cutting change reduces repeated numbers. The v2 Discussion sections retain only the most decisive numeric anchors — generally three to five per manuscript — and the v2 Conclusions are noticeably shorter and contain fewer numbers than their v1 counterparts. The Conclusion structure is now uniform across the three manuscripts: restate the question, state the answer, state the implication, stop. Many small numbers that appeared multiple times in v1 have been removed in v2, on the principle that the Discussion should refer back to Results evidence rather than re-quote it.

The fifth cross-cutting change keeps the embedding interpretation explicitly correlational. The v1 drafts were already careful here, but a few sentences risked giving the representation too much agency. In v2, embedding-related claims consistently use language such as "geometric correlate," "representation-space counterpart," "consistent with," and "aligned with the behavioural asymmetry." Wording suggesting that the embeddings explain or cause the behaviour has been removed. The caution about PCA, t-SNE, and UMAP being descriptive support only — not the basis of any numerical claim — is preserved in all three documents.

The sixth cross-cutting change improves auditability with light back-references to the Results sections. In v1, several synthesis claims in the Discussion were strong but did not point the reader back to the specific Results evidence anchoring them. In v2, each major Discussion claim now carries a brief parenthetical pointer ("Results §III.A," "Results §4.6," and so on), so a reader who wants to verify the claim against the underlying evidence can do so without searching. The pointers are kept light to avoid cluttering the prose.

## 2. Largest combined-paper-specific changes

The most consequential change to the combined manuscript is the rewrite of §V.A. In v1, this opening was a four-paragraph replay of the four Results blocks — useful as a synthesis aid for the writer but heavy as the entry point to the Discussion. In v2, §V.A is a single paragraph that states the comparative answer first, identifies the two lenses (control-versus-shift in §§V.B–V.E and representation-space evidence in §V.F) through which the answer is then defended, and points at §V.G as the protocol-bounded practical-implications layer. The numeric content of the v1 §V.A has been redistributed to the subsections that own those numbers in their argumentative role.

The second combined-specific change tightens §V.C. The v1 closing paragraph of §V.C contained language that read as ruling out optimization-schedule explanations of the nitride penalty. The v2 paragraph is more careful: it says explicitly that the penalty described is the penalty observed under the canonical Set 1 protocol, that whether a different schedule would change the magnitude or character of any of the measurement surfaces is not a question this protocol can answer either way, and that the combined claim is deliberately bounded to what the tested protocol demonstrates.

The third combined-specific change restructures §V.G — the practical-implications subsection — into two clearly labelled workflow profiles, one for chemistry-aligned targets and one for chemistry-shifted targets, with one cross-cutting implication that spans both. This change is the most visible structural difference between v1 and v2 and was made because the combined paper is the comparative manuscript of the project: its practical implications should themselves be visibly comparative. The labels "chemistry-aligned targets (the oxide profile)" and "chemistry-shifted targets (the nitride profile)" make this comparative structure unmissable.

The fourth combined-specific change shortens the Conclusion. The v1 Conclusion was three paragraphs of substantial numeric replay of the §V evidence. The v2 Conclusion has the same three paragraphs, but the middle paragraph is significantly leaner on numbers (only the most decisive anchors are retained), and the closing paragraph ends on a single sentence that names the practical meaning of chemical-family domain shift — the regime separation between chemistry-aligned and chemistry-shifted workflows — rather than re-citing measured values.

## 3. Largest nitride-specific changes

The most consequential change to the nitride manuscript is the rewrite of §5.1. In v1, this opening retraced the four-step arc of the Results section step by step before stating any interpretation. In v2, §5.1 is a single paragraph titled "The domain-shift answer in one paragraph" that opens with the interpretive answer (the cost of pretraining-target mismatch reappears at every measurement surface), then names what the four-step arc shows in compressed form, and explicitly closes with the line "the remainder of this Discussion argues that reading; it is not a replay of the four steps." This is the largest tonal change in the nitride manuscript.

The second nitride-specific change sharpens the discriminator in §5.3. The v1 §5.3 worked through the small-data versus domain-shift candidate explanations in order, with the oxide control appearing partway through the second paragraph. In v2, the section is reorganized so that the contrast against the oxide control is the very first sentence after the heading: "Nitride low-`N` inertness cannot be read as ordinary small-data noise, because oxide under the same canonical protocol does not behave this way." The discriminator is now structurally inseparable from the section's argument.

The third nitride-specific change is the rewritten lead of §5.4. The v1 §5.4 opened with a contrast between MAE-based and embedding-based questions and then unpacked three pieces of embedding evidence. In v2, §5.4 leads with the key sentence: "The embedding analysis adds two things the behavioural section cannot: a representation-space correlate of the family-level penalty, and a within-family geometric correlate of intra-family difficulty." The two contributions are then unpacked individually with their respective evidence. This makes the section's central claim immediately readable in a single sentence.

The fourth nitride-specific change ends the Conclusion more sharply. The v1 Conclusion closed with a careful but somewhat hedged paragraph about labelled-data planning. The v2 Conclusion closes with a single, directly-stated practical implication: "chemically distant targets require much more labelled data to beat a pretrained baseline under this protocol than chemistry-aligned targets do." This sentence is the protocol-bounded version of the nitride paper's headline practical message and gives the report a sharper closing line.

## 4. Largest oxide-specific changes

The most consequential change to the oxide manuscript is in §4.3. In v1, this section described oxide as the easier arm and noted that "easier" did not mean "uninformative." In v2, the section is rewritten so that the scientific role of the oxide arm is the lead claim: oxide establishes the best-case transfer regime — the regime in which the canonical protocol behaves the way the transfer-learning literature predicts — and without that reference, the nitride arm's behaviour cannot be diagnosed as domain shift rather than as small-data noise, optimizer pathology, or chemistry-specific difficulty. The section now positions the oxide arm not just as easier but as the indispensable interpretive frame for the companion nitride report.

The second oxide-specific change narrows the practical language in §§4.2 and 4.5. In v1, several sentences in these subsections — particularly the "initialization matters more than fine-tuning budget" and "zero-shot is a defensible first-pass estimator" implications — were stated more broadly than the tested regime justifies. In v2, both sentences are now scoped explicitly to the four-part regime: formation energy per atom, JARVIS `dft_3d` oxide splits, Hyperparameter Set 1, and the tested scratch sizes and N range. The point of the implication is preserved; its scope is made reviewer-safe.

The third oxide-specific change keeps the embedding section disciplined. The v1 §4.4 was already careful, but in v2 a few sentences that risked drifting toward a mechanistic argument have been tightened. The section now reads as a representation-space description that is consistent with the behavioural picture rather than as a parallel causal account, and the forward-reference to the combined manuscript's distance–error analysis is preserved as the appropriate venue for the nitride-facing mechanism analysis.

The fourth oxide-specific change adds a stronger closing sentence to the Conclusion. The v1 Conclusion closed with two sentences about the practical implication and the report's role in the broader study. In v2, the closing paragraph is rewritten to make the report's role more explicit: by establishing what "transfer working as advertised" looks like under the canonical protocol, the oxide report makes the chemical-family domain shift documented in the companion nitride work an interpretable and quantifiable phenomenon rather than an isolated observation. This gives the oxide report a clearer scientific identity within the project.

## 5. Remaining ambiguities and items needing human sign-off

Five items in the v2 drafts may benefit from human review before Phase 11 begins.

The first concerns the use of the framing "chemistry-aligned regime in which transfer works as advertised" versus "chemistry-shifted regime" in the combined paper's Conclusion and §V.G. This phrasing is consistent across v2 but was not present in v1. It captures the comparative spine of the combined paper compactly, but the human author may prefer slightly different wording — for example, "in-distribution" and "out-of-distribution" — for terminological consistency with the project brief and the standalone reports. If a different umbrella term is preferred, it should be threaded through the combined paper's §V.G headings, the Conclusion's closing sentence, and any echoes in the Introduction drafted in Phase 11.

The second item concerns the asymmetric framing of the practical-implications layer in the combined paper. The v2 §V.G is split into a chemistry-aligned profile, a chemistry-shifted profile, and one cross-cutting implication (the within-family triage signal). This three-part structure is not the only defensible layout. An alternative would keep five flat implications with explicit profile labels on each. The current v2 layout was chosen to make the comparative identity of the combined paper visible at the level of section structure, but the human author may prefer the flat layout for stylistic uniformity with the standalone reports.

The third item concerns the nitride paper's closing sentence: "chemically distant targets require much more labelled data to beat a pretrained baseline under this protocol than chemistry-aligned targets do." This is the sharpest version of the practical implication consistent with the evidence, but it carries an implicit reference to the oxide arm at the very end of the standalone nitride report. The v2 phrasing handles this implicitly by saying "under this protocol" rather than naming the oxide comparison directly. If the human author prefers a closing sentence that does not depend on the oxide context, an alternative would be: "under the canonical protocol, the labelled-data cost of moving a pretrained baseline below its own zero-shot line on chemically distant targets is substantially higher than chemistry-aligned task experience would predict."

The fourth item concerns the level of back-referencing in the Discussion sections. The v2 drafts add light parenthetical pointers to specific Results subsections — "(Results §III.A)," "(Results §4.6)," and so on — but these are kept sparse to avoid clutter. If the eventual journal target prefers heavier cross-referencing in Discussion sections (some materials journals do, others do not), the pointers can be densified during the Phase 12 assembly pass without changing any substantive prose.

The fifth item concerns the "25× the residual gap" framing in the oxide paper's §4.2 and the corresponding Conclusion language. This framing was retained from v1 because it is interpretively useful, but the precise multiplicative factor is sensitive to which residual gap is referenced (N = 500 versus N = 1000, fine-tune-vs-zero-shot versus fine-tune-vs-best-fine-tune). The v2 keeps the v1 framing exactly as written. If the human author wants a different anchor — for example, "an order of magnitude" rather than "roughly 25×" — the change is local to two sentences.

## 6. What v2 deliberately did not change

For the avoidance of doubt, the v2 rewrites preserve all of the following from v1: every measured value reported in the v1 drafts, every citation placeholder of the form `[CITE: …]`, the section numbering of all three manuscripts, the high-level subsection titles (with light wording adjustments to make them more thesis-bearing where useful), the separation of literature claims from implementation details from our findings, the use of `last_alignn_pool` as the only main-text embedding layer, the appendix-support designation of `pre_head` and `last_gcn_pool`, the nitride N = 50 caveat phrasing ("pretrained-initialization advantage over scratch, not fine-tuning adaptation"), the description of the checkpoint as "the pretrained formation-energy ALIGNN model" rather than "oxide-pretrained," and the use of "oxide-reference region" only in distance-context language.

The v2 rewrites also do not introduce any new literature claims, do not change the role assignments of the three manuscripts (oxide as control, nitride as out-of-distribution test, combined as comparison-and-mechanism), and do not change the figure or table anchors used by the prose.
