## Abstract

Transfer learning from pretrained crystal-graph neural networks is a standard strategy for reducing labelled-data requirements in materials-property prediction, but its benefit is conditional on source–target relatedness, and the behaviour of a single widely used checkpoint under a clean chemistry-family shift is not yet a standard reference point. We evaluate the pretrained formation-energy ALIGNN model distributed through the JARVIS infrastructure on two target families drawn from the JARVIS `dft_3d` benchmark: an in-distribution oxide control arm and an out-of-distribution nitride test arm. Both arms share an identical protocol: zero-shot evaluation of the pretrained checkpoint, fine-tuning across six labelled-data sizes with five random seeds per size, and matched from-scratch baselines at two of those sizes. We additionally extract frozen 256-dimensional `last_alignn_pool` embeddings and analyse family structure together with a within-family distance–error correlate. Under the canonical Hyperparameter Set 1 protocol, the pretrained checkpoint reproduces the standard chemistry-aligned picture on oxides — strong zero-shot, smooth adaptation, a large pretrained-versus-scratch advantage — and departs from it on nitrides: an approximately two-fold zero-shot penalty, a fine-tuning loop that does not leave the pretrained initialization at labelled-data sizes up to two hundred structures, and a residual gap above nitride zero-shot that persists at five hundred and one thousand structures. In the frozen representation, family labels are near-perfectly recoverable, and nitride prediction error co-varies with distance from the oxide-reference region. Scoped to the tested regime, the evidence supports a quantifiable, geometrically legible domain-shift account of when transfer remains efficient across chemistries and when a family-level penalty appears under the tested protocol.

## I. Introduction

Crystal-property prediction from atomic structure is a central workload in materials informatics. Structure-based crystal graph neural networks perform this task by learning directly from atomic coordinates, bonding topology, and element identities rather than relying on handcrafted descriptors, and they have become a routine starting point for both high-throughput screening and low-data property modelling [CITE: crystal graph baseline for materials property prediction]. CGCNN is the canonical early exemplar of the family and established that a crystal graph can be mapped directly to scalar properties without hand-engineered features [CITE: CGCNN foundational paper].

ALIGNN extends crystal-graph message passing by alternating updates on the bond graph and its line graph, making bond-angle information explicit in the learned representation and improving accuracy on structure-dependent targets including formation energy [CITE: ALIGNN foundational paper]. The specific ALIGNN checkpoint used throughout this paper — a formation-energy regressor trained on the JARVIS DFT-3D database — is distributed through the JARVIS infrastructure, which also supplies the benchmark splits used here [CITE: JARVIS dataset/repository paper].

Transfer learning from pretrained crystal-graph models has been shown to reduce labelled-data requirements on downstream property tasks, with the largest per-sample benefit typically observed in the low-data regime [CITE: transfer learning in materials informatics]. The magnitude of that benefit depends on how closely the target distribution matches the distribution that supported pretraining. When the target chemistry or structure lies outside the regime that supported pretraining, both accuracy and apparent scaling behaviour can degrade, and a growing body of out-of-distribution evaluations in materials ML makes this failure mode concrete on family-, composition-, and structure-held-out splits [CITE: domain shift or OOD benchmark in materials property prediction]. What is less standardized is a clean evaluation design built around a single, widely used pretrained checkpoint, a chemistry-family split, and a data-efficiency comparison evaluated together as a unit — zero-shot evaluation, fine-tuning across labelled-data sizes, matched from-scratch baselines, and a view of the pretrained representation — rather than any one of these surfaces in isolation. The existing literature motivates each surface but does not combine them into an isolated reference experiment.

To examine chemical-family shift under a matched protocol, we contrast an in-distribution oxide arm with an out-of-distribution nitride arm on the same pretrained formation-energy ALIGNN model [CITE: ALIGNN foundational paper] and the same JARVIS `dft_3d` benchmark splits [CITE: JARVIS dataset/repository paper]. The two arms share an identical experimental protocol: zero-shot evaluation of the pretrained checkpoint, fine-tuning across a range of labelled-data sizes at multiple random seeds per size, and matched from-scratch baselines at the two sizes where both protocols are available. To probe why the two arms may behave differently, we extract and analyse intermediate representations from the frozen pretrained model, comparing their geometry across families and asking whether within-family distance from an oxide-reference region co-varies with nitride prediction difficulty. The embedding analysis is framed as an interpretive probe consistent or inconsistent with the behavioural picture, not as a causal account of it.

The paper asks how chemical-family domain shift modulates the data-efficiency benefit of a single pretrained ALIGNN checkpoint, and whether the behavioural gap between the two families is consistent with representation-space geometry. Four linked questions structure the evaluation. RQ1 asks how the pretrained checkpoint behaves zero-shot on the oxide control task versus the nitride shifted task. RQ2 asks how fine-tuning response changes with labelled-data scale across the two arms. RQ3 asks what pretraining contributes over matched from-scratch training at the sample sizes where both protocols are available. RQ4 asks whether pretrained embedding geometry is consistent with the prediction-side difficulty gap. The four questions are treated at roughly equal weight: RQ1–RQ3 form the behavioural spine, and RQ4 is the representation-space interpretive follow-up. Section II describes the dataset, splits, protocols, and hyperparameter settings. Section III reports the oxide control results; Section IV reports the nitride results; Section V directly compares the two arms; and Section VI reports the embedding analysis as a distinct representation-space correlate section. Section VII discusses the combined evidence and its limitations, and Section VIII concludes.

## II. Methodology

### 2.1 Study design, dataset, and prediction target

We evaluate a single pretrained formation-energy ALIGNN model [CITE: ALIGNN foundational paper] on two family-specific arms that share every procedural choice other than the identity of the retained structures. The oxide arm serves as the in-distribution control and the nitride arm as the cross-family test; this contrast is a study-design choice made at the level of the project rather than a literature claim. Crystal structures and supervised targets are drawn from the JARVIS materials repository, specifically the `dft_3d_2021` release [CITE: JARVIS 2020 dataset/repository paper]. The supervised target in both arms is per-atom formation energy `formation_energy_peratom`, reported in eV/atom. Family-level summaries are stored at `data_shared/oxide/summaries/summary.json` and `data_shared/nitride/summaries/summary.json`; the script that builds both family datasets is `scripts/dataset/build_res201_family_datasets.py`.

### 2.2 Family definitions and filtering

Family assignment is element-presence based and is performed by `scripts/dataset/res201_stage2_lib.py` through `classify_family(...)` with `keep_oxynitrides_in_oxide=True`. The oxide arm contains every structure whose composition includes oxygen and retains 499 oxynitrides (structures containing both O and N). The nitride arm contains every structure whose composition includes nitrogen but not oxygen, and excludes all oxynitrides by construction (zero retained). The filter is asymmetric by design: it keeps the oxide arm fully populated with every oxygen-containing structure while guaranteeing that the nitride arm is free of mixed-anion cases.

### 2.3 Split inheritance and run-local subset construction

[INSERT TABLE TAB_METHODS_DATASET_SPLITS HERE]

The original JARVIS benchmark split identities (`provided:manifests/dft_3d_formation_energy_peratom_splits.csv`) are preserved before family filtering, so each retained structure carries its existing train, validation, or test assignment into the family arm [CITE: JARVIS 2020 dataset/repository paper]. After filtering, the oxide family contains 14 991 structures (11 960 / 1 547 / 1 484 train / val / test, pool = 13 507) and the nitride family contains 2 288 structures (1 837 / 209 / 242, pool = 2 046). For each family, the test set remains fixed; the pool is the reservoir from which run-local subsets are drawn. Full counts appear in Table 1 (`TAB_METHODS_DATASET_SPLITS`).

Run-local datasets are built by `scripts/shared/Prepare_Week1_Finetuning_Dataset.py`. For each family, each sample size `N`, and each seed, `N` structures are drawn from the family pool using `np.random.default_rng(seed).permutation(...)[:N]`; validation allocation is `n_val = max(5, round(0.1 × N))`, with training taking the remainder and a fallback of `max(1, N // 5)` when that rule would produce `n_val ≥ N`. The fixed family test set is appended unchanged to every run-local dataset, and row ordering inside each `id_prop.csv` is enforced with `keep_data_order=True` together with explicit `n_train`, `n_val`, and `n_test` arguments to preserve a deterministic load-time sequence.

### 2.4 Zero-shot baseline

Zero-shot evaluation (`scripts/shared/Evaluate_ALIGNN_Zero_Shot.py`) applies the pretrained formation-energy ALIGNN model to each family's fixed test set without task-specific training [CITE: ALIGNN foundational paper]. The script loads POSCAR files from each family's `test.csv` manifest and obtains per-structure predictions via `alignn.pretrained.get_prediction(model_name="jv_formation_energy_peratom_alignn", atoms=atoms)`; test MAE is computed with `sklearn.metrics.mean_absolute_error`. Per-structure outputs are written to `Results_Before_Correction/<family>/zero_shot/predictions.csv`, and the canonical summary is recorded in `reports/zero_shot/zero_shot_summary.csv`. Zero-shot evaluation is performed once per family and is not seed-varied.

### 2.5 Partial fine-tuning protocol

Fine-tuning (`scripts/shared/Fine_Tune_Last_Two_ALIGNN_Layers.py`) begins from the pretrained checkpoint `jv_formation_energy_peratom_alignn/checkpoint_300.pt` and its accompanying configuration. All parameters are frozen; training is then selectively re-enabled for the final gated GCN block (`gcn_layers.3`) and the scalar output head (`fc`). The overall model remains in `eval()` mode; only the two unfrozen modules are switched to training mode, and runtime invariant checks confirm that no additional parameters become trainable. Optimization uses `AdamW` with a `OneCycleLR` schedule and L1Loss. The inherited configuration JSON retains a `criterion: "mse"` entry from the original pretraining configuration that the custom training script overrides, training with L1 throughout; we record this discrepancy explicitly for reproducibility. The best checkpoint per run is selected by minimum validation L1, and each run writes a `summary.json` recording `best_epoch` and `test_mae_eV_per_atom`. The per-`N` mean of `best_epoch` over the five seeds is referred to in subsequent tables as `mean_best_epoch`; this quantity is defined here as a recorded protocol-level summary, with interpretation reserved for Results.

### 2.6 From-scratch comparison protocol

From-scratch training (`scripts/shared/Train_ALIGNN_From_Scratch.py`) reuses the identical run-local dataset roots and model configuration but instantiates a fresh ALIGNN model (`ALIGNN(ALIGNNConfig(**cfg.model.model_dump()))`) with no pretrained weights and all parameters trainable. The optimizer, learning-rate schedule, L1 objective, and best-checkpoint rule match the fine-tuning setup. For both families, from-scratch baselines exist only at `N = 50` and `N = 500`; each of the four conditions uses five seeds (20 from-scratch runs in total, 10 per family). Because no from-scratch baseline exists at `N = 10, 100, 200`, or `1 000`, every transfer-benefit claim in the paper is scoped accordingly.

### 2.7 Hyperparameter setting and model specification

[INSERT TABLE TAB_METHODS_EXPERIMENT_SCOPE HERE]

All trained runs in both arms use Hyperparameter Set 1 — the canonical namespace fixed by the project brief — with `epochs = 50`, `batch_size = 16`, and `learning_rate = 1 × 10⁻⁴`. The shared graph-construction settings are `neighbor_strategy = "k-nearest"`, `cutoff = 8.0` Å, `cutoff_extra = 3.0` Å, and `max_neighbors = 12`, with `use_canonize`, `compute_line_graph`, and `use_lmdb` enabled. The architecture is the ALIGNN formation-energy model: four ALIGNN convolutional layers, four gated GCN layers, hidden size 256, and a scalar output head [CITE: ALIGNN foundational paper]. The full experimental scope across both arms — two zero-shot evaluations, 60 fine-tuning runs, and 20 from-scratch runs — appears in Table 2 (`TAB_METHODS_EXPERIMENT_SCOPE`).

### 2.8 Evaluation metric and repeated-run summarization

The primary reported metric is test-set mean absolute error in eV/atom. Checkpoint selection for all trained protocols uses the lowest validation L1. Multi-seed results are summarized as mean ± standard deviation across the five random seeds, referred to in the main text as "five random seeds"; implementation and appendix material may name the explicit seeds `{0, 1, 2, 3, 4}`. Zero-shot values are single evaluations on the fixed family test sets and are reported without a dispersion estimate.

### 2.9 Frozen-embedding analysis

Structure embeddings were extracted from the frozen pretrained ALIGNN model (checkpoint `jv_formation_energy_peratom_alignn/checkpoint_300.pt`) without retraining and without modifying any fine-tuning or from-scratch outputs. Three layers were extracted: `last_alignn_pool`, the pooled node tensor returned by the last ALIGNN convolutional block, used throughout main-text analysis; `pre_head`, the pooled output of `model.readout` immediately before `model.fc`; and `last_gcn_pool`, the pooled node tensor from the last gated GCN block (`gcn_layers[3]`). In the current outputs, `pre_head` and `last_gcn_pool` are numerical near-duplicates of each other and are treated as a matched pair of appendix-facing supporting layers, not as independent probes.

Three fixed evaluation subsets support the embedding analyses. The `fixed_test_set` is the union of all oxide and nitride test structures; it is used for family-separation scoring and for nitride-side error metadata. The `balanced_pool_set` pairs every nitride pool structure with an equal-size random oxide pool sample (seed 42) and is used for balanced visualization and for bootstrap-resampled family-separation estimates. The `oxide_reference_pool` comprises all oxide pool structures and provides the reference manifold against which nitride embedding distances are measured.

Family separation is quantified directly in the raw 256-dimensional embedding space. For each fixed subset we compute the silhouette score, the Davies–Bouldin index, the family purity of the 15-nearest-neighbor graph, and the AUC of a logistic-regression family classifier evaluated under five-fold cross-validation with `StandardScaler` preprocessing. Raw-space statistics are the inferential layer of the analysis; PCA, t-SNE, and UMAP projections are descriptive visual support only, with primary settings `perplexity = 30` (t-SNE) and `n_neighbors = 30`, `min_dist = 0.1` (UMAP), both fitted on standardized inputs without PCA pre-reduction. PCA is fitted on the standardized `balanced_pool_set` and used to project the other subsets into that basis. Parameter sensitivity runs and overlay-fit policies are documented in `embedding_methods_appendix_notes_v1.md`.

For the distance-versus-error analysis, each nitride test structure is characterized by three embedding-space distances to the oxide-reference manifold: the Euclidean distance to the oxide centroid, the mean Euclidean distance to its five nearest oxide-reference neighbours, and a supplemental Ledoit–Wolf-regularized Mahalanobis distance evaluated only when covariance-screening diagnostics pass. These distances are paired with per-structure absolute zero-shot error. Hard nitrides are defined as the top 20 % of fixed-test nitrides by absolute zero-shot error and easy nitrides as the bottom 20 %. Statistical defaults are 1 000 bootstrap resamples for family-separation estimates, 5 000 bootstrap resamples and 10 000 permutations for distance–error tests, and within-statistic Benjamini–Hochberg false-discovery-rate adjustment. The embedding workflow is treated throughout as an interpretive protocol, not as a mechanism-proving instrument.

### 3.1 The pretrained zero-shot MAE is the best observed oxide configuration under Set 1

We evaluated the unmodified pretrained formation-energy ALIGNN checkpoint (`jv_formation_energy_peratom_alignn`) on the 1 484-structure oxide test set without any target-family fine-tuning, establishing the control benchmark against which every fine-tuning, parity, and from-scratch result reported below is compared. We make no separate claim about the exact chemical composition of the pretraining corpus, and we do not claim that no alternative fine-tuning protocol could improve on this baseline — such claims are out of scope for the Set 1 main narrative.

Three features of the trajectory stand out. First, every fine-tuning row sits above the 0.0342 eV/atom zero-shot benchmark, with positive gaps ranging from 0.0075 to 0.0181 eV/atom. Second, the `N = 10` row has a mean best epoch of 1.0, indicating that validation error did not meaningfully improve during fine-tuning and the retained checkpoint is effectively the pretrained initialization; this row is therefore a near-pretrained-checkpoint view rather than evidence of low-data adaptation, and it is flagged as `zero_shot_checkpoint_at_low_N` in the canonical numbers file. Genuine multi-epoch optimization begins at `N = 50`, where mean best epoch rises to 18.5, and stabilizes at 35–39 epochs from `N = 200` onward. Third, cross-seed variability tightens substantially as `N` grows: per-seed standard deviation falls from 0.0111 at `N = 10` to 0.0053 at `N = 1 000`, the clearest trend in the oxide fine-tuning table.

The trajectory is consistent with a small-data penalty at `N = 50` — the first point at which real gradient updates occur on a training set not yet large enough to re-match the pretrained representation's quality — followed by a monotonic recovery from `N = 100` onward. The convergence target of that recovery is the zero-shot benchmark itself, not a better-than-benchmark minimum. Under Set 1, oxide fine-tuning does not surpass zero-shot: the `N = 10` result should not be read as successful low-data adaptation, and the late-`N` decrements shrink (`N = 500 → N = 1 000` improves the mean by 0.0013 eV/atom) while variability continues to narrow — consistent with the learning curve flattening as it approaches the zero-shot benchmark from above. We describe this as flattening, not as formal saturation: only six `N` values are tested and no fine-tuning data exist above `N = 1 000`.

### 3.4 Pretrained initialization dominates random initialization at both scratch-tested sizes

[INSERT TABLE TAB_S1_FS_SUMMARY HERE]
[INSERT FIGURE FIG_S1_COMP_OXIDE HERE]

Sections 3.1–3.3 established that the pretrained checkpoint is already strong on oxides under Set 1 and that Set 1 fine-tuning does not surpass it. That alone would not demonstrate transfer value. The oxide arm's transfer-value evidence lives in this subsection: if pretrained initialization were not itself responsible for the strong oxide performance, it should be possible to reach similar error by training the same architecture from scratch on the same oxide data under the same protocol. It is not — by a very large margin.

At `N = 50` and `N = 500` — the two oxide training sizes for which from-scratch baselines were run — we compare the Set 1 fine-tuning MAE against the MAE of randomly-initialized ALIGNN models trained on the same oxide splits with the same hyperparameters, protocol, and test set (Table `TAB_S1_FS_SUMMARY`, Figure `FIG_S1_COMP_OXIDE`):

| `N` | Fine-tune mean MAE ± SD (eV/atom) | From-scratch mean MAE ± SD (eV/atom) | Scratch − fine-tune (eV/atom) | Scratch − zero-shot (eV/atom) |
|----:|----------------------------------:|-------------------------------------:|------------------------------:|------------------------------:|
| 50  | 0.0523 ± 0.0148                   | 0.5561 ± 0.0523                      | +0.5038                       | +0.5219                       |
| 500 | 0.0430 ± 0.0062                   | 0.2643 ± 0.0228                      | +0.2214                       | +0.2301                       |

At `N = 50`, from-scratch MAE is roughly an order of magnitude higher than fine-tune MAE; at `N = 500`, roughly six times higher. Between these two points, from-scratch MAE drops substantially (0.5561 → 0.2643 eV/atom) while fine-tune MAE changes only modestly (0.0523 → 0.0430 eV/atom), narrowing the transfer-benefit gap from 0.5038 to 0.2214 eV/atom. Both from-scratch points sit far above the zero-shot benchmark as well (scratch − zero-shot of 0.5219 and 0.2301 eV/atom).

The pattern is consistent with pretrained initialization providing a large labelled-data saving relative to random initialization in the oxide regime studied, and with randomly-initialized ALIGNN improving at a faster rate with more data than fine-tuning does, because fine-tuning is already bounded near the zero-shot benchmark. This gap is the oxide arm's clearest on-family signature of transfer value: fine-tuning does not need to surpass zero-shot for the pretrained representation to be delivering a substantial benefit. At `N = 500` under Set 1, fine-tuning sits 0.2214 eV/atom below scratch while sitting only 0.0088 eV/atom above zero-shot, so the transfer gain relative to a no-pretraining world is roughly 25× the residual gap to the zero-shot benchmark. The gap is measured at only two data sizes; we do not extrapolate a continuous scratch learning curve or claim where, if anywhere, scratch would intersect fine-tune or zero-shot at much larger `N`.

### 3.5 Oxide embeddings form a cohesive, locally pure region in pretrained representation space

We extract 256-dimensional pretrained embeddings at the `last_alignn_pool` layer for the fixed oxide and nitride test sets and compute raw-space family-separation metrics: silhouette score (overall and per family), Davies–Bouldin index, 15-nearest-neighbour family purity, and logistic-regression family AUC. All quantitative claims in this subsection derive from these raw 256-D statistics; PCA, t-SNE, and UMAP panels (`FIG_EA_6A_PCA`, `FIG_EA_6B_TSNE`, `FIG_EA_6C_UMAP`) serve only as descriptive visual support.

In the `last_alignn_pool` layer, oxide points form a cohesive, locally pure region in pretrained representation space. The 15-NN family purity on oxide test points is **0.9872** — oxide local neighbourhoods in this layer are almost entirely oxide-only — and the oxide silhouette is **0.2546**, higher than the nitride silhouette of 0.1453 (Table `TAB_EA_FAMILY_SEPARATION`). Across the full test set, family labels are recoverable from the raw embeddings with near-perfect separability: logistic-regression family AUC is **0.9994** and overall 15-NN family purity is 0.9655.

Three oxide-specific statements are supported by these numbers: (i) oxide local neighbourhoods in pretrained space have negligible cross-family contamination; (ii) the oxide region is internally more coherent than the nitride region; and (iii) the pretrained representation already organizes the test set along the oxide/non-oxide axis without any supervision on that axis. These descriptive observations are consistent with the behavioural picture in §§3.1–3.4: the pretrained checkpoint handles oxides well zero-shot, Set 1 fine-tuning cannot improve on that, and pretrained initialization delivers a very large advantage over scratch. The embedding evidence supports a self-contained representation-level finding — in the pretrained `last_alignn_pool` layer, oxides occupy a tight, locally pure, family-recoverable region — but the evidence is descriptive and correlational. We do not claim it causes the behavioural results in §§3.1–3.4. Quantitative distance–error analyses relating representation geometry to prediction error are nitride-facing and are reported in the nitride and combined manuscripts. Two-dimensional projections are visualization only; no numerical claim here depends on them.

## 3.6 Summary of oxide results

In descending order of scientific weight:

1. **Pretrained initialization dominates random initialization on oxides at both scratch-tested sizes.** The transfer-benefit gap is 0.5038 eV/atom at `N = 50` and 0.2214 eV/atom at `N = 500`, far larger than the residual gap between fine-tuning and the zero-shot benchmark. This is the oxide arm's central on-oxide evidence that transfer is real and valuable on the chemistry-aligned control task.
2. **The pretrained zero-shot MAE of 0.0342 eV/atom on 1 484 oxide test structures is the best observed oxide performance under Set 1 in this study.** Every fine-tuning row sits above it; every from-scratch row sits far above it.
3. **Set 1 fine-tuning converges toward, but does not surpass, the zero-shot benchmark.** After an `N = 10` near-pretrained-checkpoint row and an `N = 50` small-data penalty, mean test MAE recovers monotonically to 0.0417 eV/atom at `N = 1 000` and becomes substantially more reproducible as cross-seed standard deviation narrows from 0.0111 to 0.0053 eV/atom.
4. **The pretrained `last_alignn_pool` representation places oxides in a cohesive, locally pure region** (15-NN family purity 0.9872; oxide silhouette 0.2546; family AUC 0.9994) — a representation-level finding consistent with the above but not a causal explanation of it.

Together, these results constitute the oxide arm's disciplined control evidence: pretraining delivers a very large advantage over scratch on an in-distribution target, the pretrained benchmark is already strong and is not surpassed under Set 1 fine-tuning, and the pretrained representation organizes oxides into a tight, well-separated region consistent with that behaviour. These findings set the reference condition against which the nitride arm's domain-shift analysis is carried out in the later sections of this paper.

## IV. Results II — Nitride Domain-Shift Results

### Scope and caveats

This section reports behavioural evidence (zero-shot, fine-tuning, from-scratch) and representational evidence (frozen `last_alignn_pool` embedding) on the fixed 242-structure nitride test split, with matched oxide comparator values where oxide serves as the in-distribution control. All fine-tuning and from-scratch results use Hyperparameter Set 1 (50 epochs, learning rate 1 × 10⁻⁴, batch size 16, five seeds per configuration). Zero-shot uses the pretrained formation-energy ALIGNN model without target-family training.

Four caveats apply throughout and are referenced economically below: **(C1)** we do not describe the pretrained checkpoint as "oxide-pretrained"; **(C2)** nitride `N ≤ 200` is not meaningful adaptation, because `mean_best_epoch = 1.0` at every seed; **(C3)** the `N = 50` scratch gap reflects pretrained-initialization advantage, not fine-tuning adaptation, because under **C2** the fine-tuned side is itself the zero-shot checkpoint; **(C4)** embedding distance is reported as a correlational geometric indicator, not as a cause of prediction error.

The section follows a four-step domain-shift arc. Step 1 (§4.1) is the zero-shot penalty. Step 2 (§4.2) is low-`N` fine-tuning inertness under the canonical protocol. Step 3 (§4.3) is genuine but incomplete adaptation at high `N`. Step 4 (§§4.4–4.5) is embedding-space geometry consistent with the persistence of that penalty. A supporting pretrained-vs-scratch comparison (§4.6) confirms pretraining's operational value on nitrides but is secondary to the Step 1–4 arc; §4.7 summarizes.

### 4.1 Step 1 — zero-shot evaluation establishes a nitride penalty relative to oxides

On matched evaluation, the pretrained formation-energy ALIGNN checkpoint attains a test MAE of **0.0695 eV/atom** on the fixed nitride test set (n = 242) and **0.0342 eV/atom** on the oxide comparator (n = 1 484) — a nitride error roughly twice the oxide error (Table `TAB_ZS_SUMMARY`, Figure `FIG_ZS_COMPARISON`). The pretrained formation-energy ALIGNN model therefore incurs a measurable accuracy penalty on nitrides relative to the oxide control before any target-family adaptation — the first step of the domain-shift arc and the reference baseline against which every later nitride fine-tuning comparison must be judged. §§4.2–4.3 evaluate whether fine-tuning on target-family data closes this gap; §§4.4–4.5 ask whether the representation shows a geometric correlate of it.

This subsection does not attribute the gap to any specific chemical or graph-topological feature; attribution is examined in §§4.4–4.5 via embedding analysis. Under **C1**, the checkpoint is described as the *pretrained formation-energy ALIGNN model* because the JARVIS-DFT pretraining corpus is broad rather than oxide-exclusive; its composition is consistent with a pretraining regime more aligned with oxides than nitrides.

### 4.2 Step 2 — low-`N` nitride fine-tuning is operationally inert under the canonical protocol

We compare mean test MAE across five seeds at `N ∈ {10, 50, 100, 200}` against the nitride zero-shot MAE of 0.0695 eV/atom and against the analogous oxide fine-tuning rows at matched `N` (Table `TAB_S1_FT_SUMMARY_BY_N`; low-`N` portion of `FIG_S1_LC_NITRIDE`). At every one of the four smallest nitride budgets, `mean_best_epoch = 1.0`: 0.0874 ± 0.0199 eV/atom at `N = 10`, 0.1173 ± 0.0451 at `N = 50`, 0.1722 ± 0.0996 at `N = 100`, and 0.1392 ± 0.0677 at `N = 200`. Within the resolution of this protocol, the returned model is indistinguishable from the pretrained checkpoint with a single optimizer step applied (**C2**). Under Hyperparameter Set 1, nitride fine-tuning at `N ≤ 200` therefore does not constitute adaptation in any operational sense; the numerically smallest mean MAE in this band (0.0874 eV/atom at `N = 10`) is not a "best low-`N` fine-tuning result" but an early-checkpoint artifact, and it still sits 0.0179 eV/atom above zero-shot. This is Step 2 of the domain-shift arc: an inert low-`N` fine-tuning regime in which the loop does not leave the pretrained starting point.

Unlike oxide, which begins genuine optimization by `N = 50` (`mean_best_epoch = 18.5`, rising to 35.5–39.0 at `N ≥ 200`), nitride remains operationally inert across the full low-`N` band up to `N = 200`. The asymmetry is in *when* multi-epoch optimization engages at all, not only in final MAE. Two mechanisms are consistent with the flat epoch-1 pattern within the observed summary: validation loss may genuinely stop improving after the first step because the pretrained checkpoint is a better initialization than any nearby point reachable in one epoch on the given budget, or the small validation splits at low `N` may be too coarse to discriminate between neighbouring checkpoints. Either is compatible with the data; we do not select between them here.

From-scratch nitride baselines exist only at `N = 50` and `N = 500`, so no continuous transfer-benefit curve is inferred across `N`.

## 4.7 Summary — the four-step domain-shift arc

Taken together, the nitride evidence produces a single, internally consistent four-step story.

**Step 1 (§4.1).** Zero-shot evaluation places nitrides at roughly 2× the oxide-comparator MAE at the pretrained starting point (0.0695 vs 0.0342 eV/atom).

**Step 2 (§4.2).** Under Set 1, fine-tuning at `N ≤ 200` is operationally inert (`mean_best_epoch = 1.0` at every seed across all four sizes). Oxide, by contrast, has already begun genuine optimization by `N = 50`.

**Step 3 (§4.3).** At `N = 500` (mean best epoch 40.5) and `N = 1 000` (mean best epoch 45.0), fine-tuning becomes genuine and tighter across seeds, but no tested budget recovers the zero-shot baseline; the `N = 1 000` mean test MAE (0.0907 eV/atom) still sits 0.0211 eV/atom above zero-shot.

**Step 4 (§§4.4–4.5).** In frozen `last_alignn_pool` space, families are distinguishable but the nitride region is less cohesive than the oxide control region, and within nitrides, distance from the oxide-reference region co-varies with absolute zero-shot error (Spearman ρ = 0.3428, FDR `q = 1.3 × 10⁻⁴`; hard-minus-easy gap 0.8168, FDR `q = 1.8 × 10⁻⁴`).

**Supporting layer (§4.6).** Pretrained initialization outperforms random initialization by a wide margin at `N = 50` and `N = 500` (under the `N = 50` initialization-advantage caveat **C3**); this confirms pretraining's operational value but does not revise the headline finding that the domain-shift penalty survives genuine adaptation.

## V. Results III — Direct oxide–nitride comparison

Results I and Results II established the oxide and nitride arms separately. This section places the two families on a single comparative footing using three axes that together characterize the behavioural domain-shift penalty: the zero-shot starting point, the fine-tuning response across training-set sizes, and the transfer benefit of pretrained initialization over from-scratch training. All comparisons in this section use the same pretrained formation-energy ALIGNN checkpoint [CITE: ALIGNN_PAPER], the same JARVIS `dft_3d` split logic [CITE: JARVIS_INFRA], the same canonical hyperparameter set (`epochs = 50`, `lr = 1e-4`, `batch = 16`), and the same fixed oxide and nitride test sets (1 484 and 242 structures, respectively). Parity-plot MAE values reported here are on-figure values computed from seed-averaged predictions and therefore differ slightly from the summary MAE values in Table `TAB_S1_FT_SUMMARY_BY_N`, which are computed as per-seed MAE averages.

### III.A. Zero-shot family gap

[INSERT TABLE TAB_ZS_SUMMARY HERE]
[INSERT FIGURE FIG_ZS_COMPARISON HERE]

Evaluating the pretrained formation-energy ALIGNN checkpoint without target-family training on both family test sets isolates the behavioural starting point of transfer, before any target-family signal has been seen. Figure `FIG_ZS_COMPARISON` reports the same-checkpoint zero-shot MAE as 0.0342 eV/atom on oxides (n = 1 484) and 0.0695 eV/atom on nitrides (n = 242): the nitride error exceeds the oxide error by 0.0354 eV/atom and is 2.03× the oxide value.

The direction of the gap is consistent with the project framing that treats oxides as in-distribution relative to the broadly JARVIS-trained checkpoint and nitrides as a chemical-family out-of-distribution test [CITE: PROJECT_BRIEF]. The test sets differ in size, so sampling uncertainty on the two zero-shot values is not identical; this affects how tightly each value is pinned but not the direction of the gap. Read conservatively, the comparison establishes a behavioural domain-shift penalty of approximately a factor of two in formation-energy MAE before any target-family fine-tuning, and characterizes the starting point from which each family's adaptation curve will be measured in §§III.B–III.C. This is a behavioural observation, not a mechanism claim: the zero-shot gap is compatible with several non-exclusive causes — distributional differences in bond chemistry, differences in pretraining coverage, or differences in intrinsic hardness of formation-energy prediction — and no causal attribution is made here. §IV introduces a correlational geometric counterpart consistent with the gap but does not explain it.

### III.B. Differential fine-tuning response across training-set sizes

[INSERT TABLE TAB_S1_FT_SUMMARY_BY_N HERE]
[INSERT FIGURE FIG_S1_LC_OXIDE HERE]
[INSERT FIGURE FIG_S1_LC_NITRIDE HERE]

We fine-tune the same pretrained checkpoint on each family at `N ∈ {10, 50, 100, 200, 500, 1 000}` labelled structures, with multiple seeds. For each family and each `N` we report mean test MAE and mean best epoch across seeds. The mean best epoch — the per-seed epoch at which validation loss reached its minimum, averaged across seeds — is a direct behavioural indicator of whether the model has entered a multi-epoch optimization regime or remained at an early-checkpoint fixed point.

The paired learning-curve panels (`FIG_S1_LC_OXIDE`, `FIG_S1_LC_NITRIDE`) and the accompanying summary (Table `TAB_S1_FT_SUMMARY_BY_N`) reveal three coupled patterns. First, at every `N` nitride mean MAE exceeds oxide mean MAE, with the family gap in mean fine-tuned MAE ranging from 0.0457 eV/atom at `N = 10` to 0.1257 eV/atom at `N = 100`. Second, oxide mean best epoch climbs from 1.0 at `N = 10` to 18.5 at `N = 50` and then to 20.0, 39.0, 39.0, and 35.5 at `N = 100, 200, 500, 1 000`. Third, nitride mean best epoch remains pinned at 1.0 across `N = 10, 50, 100, 200` and only departs from this value at `N = 500` (40.5) and `N = 1 000` (45.0). No nitride fine-tuning row beats nitride zero-shot MAE; the best genuinely-adapted nitride configuration at `N = 1 000` reaches 0.0907 eV/atom, still above the zero-shot value of 0.0695 eV/atom.

The family contrast is not only that nitride MAE is uniformly higher. It is that oxide begins genuine multi-epoch optimization by `N = 50`, whereas nitride remains operationally inert through `N = 200` — validation loss is minimized at the first epoch and does not improve over the fifty-epoch budget. The mean best epoch distinguishes these regimes more reliably than a single MAE value, because two models can have similar MAE for very different reasons: one because it has been optimized to a stable fit, the other because it has not yet moved away from its initialization. The nitride low-`N` rows should therefore be read as reports of the pretrained checkpoint at the first fine-tuning epoch, not as successful low-data adaptation; the clean cross-family comparison of adapted fine-tuning begins at `N = 500`, where both families show mean best epochs well beyond 1.0. Even at that size, and at `N = 1 000`, the family gap persists: the oxide endpoint lies close to oxide zero-shot while the nitride endpoint remains above nitride zero-shot. This is consistent with a picture in which the chemical-family shift is felt not only in the starting error but in how readily the same optimization protocol converts labelled data into adaptation.

We cannot rule out that a different hyperparameter schedule — a longer training budget, a different learning-rate schedule, or a more aggressive early-layer unfreezing policy — would push nitride fine-tuning into genuine adaptation at lower `N`. The canonical namespace used here is the project-fixed protocol and not the result of a per-family tuning search; no claim is made about alternative settings.

The transfer-benefit comparison reported here is specific to the canonical hyperparameter set, the canonical JARVIS splits, and the two tested scratch sizes; no extrapolation is made to intermediate `N`, to other optimization schedules, or to other evaluation protocols.

## VI. Results IV — Embedding analysis and interpretation of domain shift

§III characterizes the behavioural penalty using test MAE, learning-curve shape, adaptation timing, and transfer-benefit arithmetic — all outputs of the model. §IV examines the internal representation from which those outputs are computed: the penultimate pooled features of the pretrained ALIGNN checkpoint, extracted with the model frozen and no target-family training. The goal is not to replace the behavioural comparison with an embedding-based one, but to test whether the frozen representation shows structure geometrically consistent with the behavioural penalty already established. Every quantitative claim in this section is computed in the raw 256-dimensional `last_alignn_pool` space; PCA, t-SNE, and UMAP panels are descriptive visual support only and are not the basis of any numerical claim.

### IV.A. Implementation

Embeddings are extracted from the `last_alignn_pool` layer, the pooled graph-level feature immediately after the ALIGNN stack and before the formation-energy regression head. For each structure, the embedding is a raw 256-dimensional vector. **All statistical claims in this section are computed in that raw 256-D space.** Two-dimensional projections (PCA, t-SNE, UMAP) appear as descriptive figures only; their axes, inter-cluster distances, cluster sizes, and apparent densities are not metric quantities and are not used to make numerical claims. t-SNE and UMAP in particular preserve aspects of local neighbourhood structure but do not preserve global inter-cluster geometry [CITE: WEEK2_ASSIGNMENT]. The PCA projection used in the main figure explains 18.13 % of variance on PC1 and 9.47 % on PC2 (27.60 % cumulative) when fit on a balanced pool of 4 092 structures, and is therefore informative but lossy as a 2-D summary of the 256-D geometry.

The family-separation analysis uses the fixed test set of 1 726 structures (1 484 oxides, 242 nitrides). The distance–error analysis uses the full nitride test set (242 structures) and an oxide-reference pool of 13 507 oxide train+val structures. For each nitride test structure, we compute the mean Euclidean distance from its raw 256-D `last_alignn_pool` embedding to its five nearest oxide-reference embeddings; this is the "5NN oxide-reference distance" used throughout §IV.C. Statistical tests report 95 % confidence intervals (bootstrap) and FDR-adjusted `q`-values.

Raw-space metrics and behavioural MAE curves are consistent: they describe the same contrast from two different measurement surfaces. The logistic-regression AUC quantifies family-label recoverability from the frozen embeddings, not the mechanism by which formation-energy prediction errors arise; a near-perfect family AUC is compatible with many downstream error patterns. The silhouette, Davies–Bouldin, 15-NN purity, and logistic-AUC values are raw-space geometric descriptors; they do not quantify how much of the behavioural MAE gap is attributable to representation structure as opposed to label distribution, prediction-head calibration, or other non-geometric factors. The 2-D projections, again, are descriptive only.

The boxplot captures this as a clear effect-size difference at the tails, and the scatter shows that the relationship extends across the full nitride test population as a moderate positive monotonic and linear association. The relationship is correlational and non-deterministic. The hard and easy groups overlap substantially in distance, the Spearman rank correlation is moderate (0.3428), and the association is reported for absolute zero-shot error only — not for fine-tuned error, where the picture may differ. We do not claim that distance from the oxide-reference pool causes prediction error, that it is the only geometric factor associated with error, or that it applies symmetrically to oxides; the oxide-reference pool is an operational reference region defined from the heavier-represented family in the training data, not evidence that the pretrained checkpoint is oxide-exclusive.

### IV.D. What the embedding analysis adds beyond MAE and learning curves

The MAE tables and learning curves in §§III.A–III.D establish the domain-shift penalty as a behavioural property of the model: nitride zero-shot error is about 2× oxide zero-shot error, oxide optimization begins at `N = 50` while nitride optimization is delayed to `N = 500`, and the nitride family gap persists even when both families are in the adapted regime. Those results answer the question "how large is the penalty, and when does it appear?".

The embedding analysis in §§IV.B–IV.C answers a different question: does the frozen pretrained representation, examined before any target-family training, already show structure consistent with the observed penalty? It contributes three pieces of information that MAE tables and learning curves do not. First, family structure is already present in the frozen representation to the point where a linear probe recovers family labels almost perfectly (AUC 0.9994), bringing the representation itself — and not only the downstream regression head — into view as a geometric correlate of the domain-shift picture. Second, family structure is asymmetric in raw space: by every per-family raw-space metric considered, the oxide region is more internally cohesive than the nitride region, and that asymmetry aligns with the direction of the behavioural asymmetry. Third, the analysis provides a within-family geometric indicator — 5NN oxide-reference distance — that co-varies with zero-shot error across the full nitride population, supplying a geometric correlate of relative difficulty across structures that aggregate MAE cannot resolve.

None of these additions replaces the behavioural evidence, and none demonstrates causality. What the embedding analysis adds is a representation-space counterpart to the behavioural pattern, one that makes the domain-shift interpretation geometrically legible without upgrading it to a mechanism claim.

### IV.E. Synthesis of the geometric and behavioural evidence

Taken together, §§III and IV describe a consistent picture under the canonical protocol. The pretrained formation-energy ALIGNN checkpoint starts from a meaningfully higher zero-shot MAE on nitrides than on oxides, adapts slowly on nitrides relative to oxides across `N`, and does not close the family gap even at `N = 1 000`. In the frozen representation from which those outputs are computed, oxides and nitrides occupy family-distinguishable regions in raw 256-D space; the nitride region is less cohesive than the oxide region by every per-family raw-space metric considered; and within nitrides, zero-shot error co-varies with distance from the oxide-reference region. The behavioural penalty and the representation-space geometry are consistent, but the consistency is correlational rather than causal: the frozen representation is the setting in which the penalty is measured, not a proven source of it. The Discussion develops the practical implications of this combined picture for small-data transfer in materials property prediction.

## VII. Discussion

### VII.A. The comparative answer in one paragraph

Under the canonical protocol on a formation-energy target, a single pretrained ALIGNN checkpoint behaves as transfer learning predicts on the in-distribution oxide arm and only partially as predicted on the out-of-distribution nitride arm. The gap between the two arms surfaces at every measurement examined and shows a consistent geometric correlate in the frozen representation. The oxide arm reproduces the standard chemistry-aligned picture — strong zero-shot, smooth adaptation, a large pretrained-vs-scratch advantage. The nitride arm departs from that picture in a structured way: the starting error is higher, the optimizer does not move off the pretrained checkpoint at low `N` under the canonical protocol, and the family penalty persists in the residual error of the genuinely-adapted models. The frozen representation in which both behaviours are read shows family structure and a within-family geometric indicator that point in the same direction as the behavioural contrast, without proving causation. The remainder of this Discussion develops that answer through two lenses — the control-versus-shift contrast in §§VII.B–VII.E and the representation-space evidence in §VII.F — and converts it into protocol-bounded practical implications in §VII.G.

### VII.B. What pretraining helps with

Transfer learning in crystal-graph materials ML is conventionally defended as a two-part claim: pretrained representations improve target-task accuracy over random initialization on the same architecture and protocol, and they do so most strongly when target-family data are scarce [CITE: Lee & Asahi 2021; Kim et al. 2024]. The combined evidence supports the first part on both arms and qualifies the second.

Both families show a pretrained-over-scratch gap that is large in absolute terms at the two scratch-tested sizes — gaps of 0.5038 and 0.2214 eV/atom on oxides and 0.5741 and 0.2706 eV/atom on nitrides at `N = 50` and `N = 500`. The arithmetic ordering between families is not, however, interpretable as a cross-family ranking of transfer efficiency: the nitride bars are inflated by weaker scratch anchors in absolute terms, and the nitride `N = 50` bar measures pretrained-initialization advantage over scratch, not fine-tuning adaptation, because the corresponding fine-tuning row is itself the epoch-1 checkpoint under Set 1. The only clean cross-family adapted-vs-scratch cross-section under the canonical protocol is `N = 500`, where both families' fine-tuning rows show mean best epochs well above 1.0.

Two readings follow. First, the operational claim that pretraining beats random initialization on both families at the two scratch-tested sizes is supported [CITE: Lee & Asahi 2021; Kim et al. 2024; Hu et al. 2024], and the arithmetic magnitude of that benefit depends on the quality of the scratch anchor rather than on target-family adaptation quality. Second, the fine-tuning-vs-zero-shot gap is the more telling efficiency variable: on oxides it sits above zero-shot throughout the tested range and flattens toward it; on nitrides it sits above zero-shot and does not converge to it within the tested range. Pretraining therefore helps both families relative to a no-pretraining counterfactual, while fine-tuning's residual contribution on top of pretraining is small and positive on oxides and does not push nitride below its own zero-shot baseline at any tested size under the canonical protocol.

### VII.C. What chemical-family mismatch costs under the canonical protocol

Literature on OOD generalization in materials ML reports, cautiously, that family- or composition-held-out splits produce larger errors than random splits and that transfer-learning gains shrink as chemical distance between source and target grows [CITE: Omee et al. 2024; Li et al. 2025 — OOD; Li et al. 2025 — adversarial; Hu et al. 2024]. The combined evidence concretizes this picture in the specific setting of a formation-energy ALIGNN pretrained on broadly JARVIS data and evaluated on a chemically distinct target family, and it does so on three measurement surfaces.

The first cost is visible before any target-family training: the zero-shot family gap of 2.03× in test MAE is the cleanest quantitative signature of the in-distribution versus out-of-distribution split, because it removes every confound introduced by fine-tuning. The second cost is visible in the fine-tuning timing contrast. Oxide fine-tuning enters genuine multi-epoch optimization at `N = 50` (mean best epoch 18.5) and stabilizes by `N = 200` (mean best epoch 39.0); nitride fine-tuning remains pinned at mean best epoch 1.0 from `N = 10` through `N = 200` and only engages at `N = 500` (40.5) and `N = 1 000` (45.0). The asymmetry is in *when* multi-epoch optimization engages at all, a behaviour that final-MAE-only tables do not surface. The third cost is visible in the residual gap at adapted configurations: at `N = 500` and `N = 1 000`, with both families in the adapted regime, oxide mean test MAE sits within 0.01 eV/atom of oxide zero-shot, while nitride sits 0.02–0.03 eV/atom above nitride zero-shot.

The combined reading, scoped to the canonical protocol, is that chemical-family mismatch on this target is neither a transient low-data effect that more samples within the tested range would dissolve nor a single-MAE artefact. It is a consistent penalty that appears at the pretrained starting point, governs whether the optimizer moves off that starting point under Set 1, and persists in the adapted regime within the tested `N` range. Whether a different optimization schedule would change the magnitude or character of any of these surfaces is not a question the present protocol can answer; the penalty we describe is the penalty observed under the canonical Set 1 protocol.

### VII.D. Why oxide is easier

Oxides sit within the operating regime of a broadly JARVIS-trained representation by study design [CITE: Choudhary et al. 2020 — JARVIS; Choudhary & DeCost 2021 — ALIGNN]. Three empirical features of the oxide arm are consistent with this positioning without requiring any stronger claim about the chemical composition of the pretraining corpus.

The pretrained checkpoint already performs well zero-shot on the oxide test set (0.0342 eV/atom) — the best observed oxide configuration under Set 1 in this study and the expected shape of transfer on chemistry-aligned targets [CITE: Choudhary et al. 2024 — JARVIS-Leaderboard]. The fine-tuning loop willingly leaves initialization at small `N`, with `mean_best_epoch` rising to 18.5 at `N = 50` and plateauing at 35–39 from `N = 200` onward, consistent with sustained multi-epoch optimization on splits large enough to move the model. And the oxide arm is internally reproducible at scale, with cross-seed standard deviation tightening from 0.0111 at `N = 10` to 0.0053 at `N = 1 000`. Read as a control, the oxide arm's "easier" status is what makes the nitride arm interpretable: it is the regime in which the pretrained representation works as advertised under the canonical protocol, so deviations elsewhere can be measured against a concrete reference rather than against an idealized expectation.

### VII.E. Why nitride is harder

Three empirical features of the nitride arm are consistent with treating chemical distance as the operative source of difficulty under the canonical protocol [CITE: Omee et al. 2024; Li et al. 2025 — OOD; Hu et al. 2024].

Zero-shot MAE is elevated before any target-family training — 2.03× the oxide comparator on matched evaluation. Because the same checkpoint produces both numbers under identical protocol and differs only in the target family, the zero-shot gap isolates a family-level discrepancy at the pretrained starting point itself. The low-`N` inertness pattern is not a data-quantity artefact: the oxide comparator under the same Set 1 protocol and in the same low-`N` band has already entered genuine multi-epoch optimization, so if nitride inertness were only a noisy-gradient story it should appear on oxides too, and it does not. The candidate that survives this control is a domain-shift reading: the `mean_best_epoch = 1.0` signature at nitride `N ≤ 200` reads as an optimizer that cannot find a nearby point better than the pretrained initialization within the canonical 50-epoch budget, not as a failure of small-data training in general. And the residual gap persists at adapted configurations: the best adapted nitride configuration (`N = 1 000`) still sits above the nitride zero-shot baseline.

The compact nitride reading is therefore that the arm is harder not because nitrides are intrinsically harder materials to model, but because the pretrained formation-energy representation is operating outside the regime where it can cheaply adapt to small target-family budgets under the canonical protocol, and because the penalty associated with that fact persists under the tested `N` range and schedule.

### VII.F. What the embedding analysis adds beyond performance curves

MAE tables, learning curves, and parity plots are outputs of the model. They describe the domain-shift penalty as a behavioural property — its size at zero-shot, when fine-tuning begins to engage, and whether adapted fine-tuning closes the family gap. They do not localize difficulty to anything in the representation from which the model reads. The embedding analysis answers a different question: does the frozen pretrained representation, examined before any target-family training, already show structure consistent with the behavioural penalty? [CITE: Omee et al. 2024; Li et al. 2025 — OOD; Li et al. 2025 — adversarial]

The answer is three-part (Results §IV). First, family structure is already present in the frozen representation to the point where a linear probe recovers family labels from raw 256-D `last_alignn_pool` embeddings almost perfectly (logistic-regression family AUC 0.9994). The pretrained network has organized the test set along the oxide/nitride axis without supervision on that axis, bringing the pretrained representation — not only the downstream regression head — into view as a geometric correlate of the family contrast. Second, family structure is asymmetric in raw space: by per-family silhouette and per-family 15-NN purity, the oxide region is more internally cohesive than the nitride region, and that asymmetry aligns in direction with the behavioural asymmetry. Third, the embedding analysis provides a within-family geometric indicator of relative difficulty that aggregate MAE cannot resolve: 5-nearest-oxide distance co-varies with nitride zero-shot error across the full nitride test population (Spearman ρ = 0.3428, FDR `q = 1.3 × 10⁻⁴`), with the hard-versus-easy distance gap stable under alternative distance definitions reported in the appendix.

None of these additions replaces the behavioural evidence, and none demonstrates causality. What the embedding analysis adds is a representation-space counterpart to the behavioural pattern — a geometric correlate that makes the domain-shift interpretation legible without upgrading it to a mechanism claim. Shared upstream factors — bonding chemistry, coordination environment, local symmetry — may jointly drive both displacement from the oxide-reference region and the unreliability of the pretrained head on particular structures [CITE: Omee et al. 2024; Li et al. 2025 — adversarial]. PCA, t-SNE, and UMAP projections are descriptive visual support only; visual inter-cluster distances are not quoted as statistical evidence, because such projections preserve local structure but not global inter-cluster geometry in a physically interpretable way [CITE: van der Maaten & Hinton 2008; McInnes et al. 2018]. Every quantitative claim in the embedding section is computed in the raw 256-D `last_alignn_pool` space.

### VII.G. Practical implications for small-data materials discovery

The combined evidence yields two distinct workflow profiles — one for chemistry-aligned targets and one for chemistry-shifted targets — and one indicator that spans both. Each is scoped explicitly to the regime tested (formation energy per atom, JARVIS `dft_3d` splits, the pretrained formation-energy ALIGNN checkpoint, Hyperparameter Set 1, six `N` values up to 1 000) and, where relevant, to the two scratch-tested sizes.

*Chemistry-aligned targets (the oxide profile).* In this regime, the choice of initialization is the dominant data-efficiency lever: at `N = 500` the pretrained-vs-scratch gap is 0.2214 eV/atom on oxides, while the difference between `N = 500` and `N = 1 000` fine-tuning means is roughly 0.001 eV/atom. If a project budget must be spent on one decision, spending it on obtaining a strong pretrained starting point is a much better investment than spending it on additional labelled target-family data, within the tested `N` range. Pretrained zero-shot is also a defensible first-pass estimator on chemistry-aligned formation-energy targets in a comparable regime: under Set 1 it is the best observed oxide configuration in this study, and fine-tuning is most useful for tightening cross-seed reproducibility (per-seed standard deviation roughly halves across the tested `N` range) rather than for producing the headline accuracy.

*Chemistry-shifted targets (the nitride profile).* On chemically distant targets in a comparable regime, a few hundred labelled target-family structures should not be counted on to beat the pretrained zero-shot baseline under a standard fine-tuning protocol. The tested range up to `N = 1 000` does not produce a nitride fine-tuning configuration below nitride zero-shot under Set 1, and under the canonical protocol the threshold at which the optimizer engages at all sits between `N = 200` and `N = 500`. Project planning on comparable targets should adopt this as the default expectation rather than the exception. On shifted targets, the pretrained-vs-scratch gap — not the fine-tune-vs-zero-shot residual — is the more defensible measure of transfer value, because in this regime the residual is negative; the order-of-magnitude transfer claim (0.2706 eV/atom at `N = 500` on nitrides) is the robust one.

*Spanning both profiles.* Pretrained-space geometry can serve as a within-family triage signal on shifted tasks: the moderate but significant correlation between 5-nearest-oxide distance and absolute zero-shot error (Spearman ρ = 0.3428, FDR `q = 1.3 × 10⁻⁴`) suggests a representation-based hardness score for allocating additional labelling effort or uncertainty estimation. We stop short of proposing this as a deployment-ready tool: the correlation is moderate, hard and easy distance distributions overlap substantially, and the association is reported for absolute zero-shot error only.

### VII.H. Limitations

Six limitations bound the combined claim. *(i) Protocol scope.* All results are reported under Hyperparameter Set 1; we do not claim that nitride fine-tuning is incapable of beating zero-shot under alternative schedules, only that it does not within the tested protocol and `N` range. The nitride low-`N` inertness is documented under canonical Set 1. *(ii) Scratch coverage.* From-scratch baselines exist only at `N = 50` and `N = 500` for both families; under the nitride `N = 50` initialization-advantage caveat the only clean cross-family adapted-vs-scratch cross-section is `N = 500`, and no continuous transfer-benefit curve is inferred. *(iii) Fine-tuning range.* Six `N` values were tested up to `N = 1 000`; we do not claim demonstrated saturation on oxides or a demonstrated floor on nitrides at larger `N`. *(iv) Test-set sizes.* The oxide test set contains 1 484 structures and the nitride test set 242; sampling uncertainty on the two zero-shot values is not identical, and the 2× family-gap statement is directional rather than tightly pinned. *(v) Checkpoint scope.* The checkpoint is the pretrained formation-energy ALIGNN model distributed through the JARVIS infrastructure; we do not characterize its training corpus chemically and deliberately do not describe it as "oxide-pretrained." "Oxide-reference region" is used only in the distance-context language of Results IV and §VII.F. *(vi) Embedding causality.* Raw-space `last_alignn_pool` metrics and the distance–error association are descriptive and correlational; they do not quantify how much of the behavioural MAE gap is attributable to representation geometry, and they do not constitute mechanism proof.

### VII.I. Future work

Four directions extend the combined analysis. Per-family hyperparameter tuning would quantify how much of the nitride low-`N` inertness is protocol-contingent under the canonical evaluation conditions; lower learning rates, longer budgets, or discriminative schedules could plausibly move `mean_best_epoch` off 1.0 at `N ≤ 200`. Scratch baselines at `N ∈ {10, 100, 200, 1 000}` in both families would convert the present two-point pretrained-vs-scratch comparison into a continuous transfer-benefit curve and would allow the nitride `N = 50` caveat to be benchmarked against neighbouring sizes. Extending the distance–error analysis from zero-shot error to fine-tuned error at `N = 500` and `N = 1 000` would test whether the geometric correlate of difficulty survives adaptation or relaxes with it, and whether the within-family indicator generalizes to the adapted regime. A compositional, coordination-environment, or structural-prototype decomposition of the nitride residual at high `N` would test which subpopulations drive the parity dispersion and the tail of the distance–error distribution, beginning to move the embedding view from a geometric correlate toward a chemistry-interpretable one.

## VIII. Conclusion

This paper asked how a pretrained formation-energy ALIGNN model behaves on an in-distribution oxide control task versus an out-of-distribution nitride test task, and whether pretrained embedding-space geometry could help interpret the difference in adaptation difficulty.

The combined evidence supports a single control-versus-shift answer. On the in-distribution control task, the pretrained checkpoint is already strong, canonical fine-tuning converges toward but does not cross that benchmark, and pretrained initialization beats random initialization by a wide margin at both scratch-tested sizes. On the out-of-distribution test task under the canonical protocol, the same checkpoint incurs a 2× family-level zero-shot penalty; the canonical fine-tuning loop is operationally inert at `N ≤ 200` — `mean_best_epoch = 1.0` across all seeds, so the selected checkpoint is effectively the pretrained zero-shot state — and the genuinely-adapted configurations at `N = 500` and `N = 1 000` remain above the nitride zero-shot baseline. In the frozen `last_alignn_pool` representation from which both behaviours are computed, family labels are near-perfectly recoverable, the nitride region is less cohesive than the oxide region, and within nitrides, zero-shot error co-varies with distance from the oxide-reference region — a correlational geometric indicator consistent with a representation-space shift, not a causal mechanism proof.

The practical reading is that the choice of pretrained initialization is the dominant data-efficiency lever on both families in the regime tested; that zero-shot is a defensible first-pass estimator on chemistry-aligned targets and a non-negotiable baseline on chemically distant ones; and that pretrained-space geometry can serve as a modest within-family triage indicator on shifted tasks.

Read together, these findings position chemical-family domain shift not as a failure mode of transfer learning in materials ML but as a quantifiable, geometrically legible cost. Under the canonical protocol, that cost separates the chemistry-aligned regime in which transfer works as advertised from the chemistry-shifted regime in which the labelled-data cost of moving below a pretrained baseline is substantially higher than chemistry-aligned task experience would predict — a cost that should be planned for rather than discovered after the fact.
