## Results III. Direct oxide–nitride comparison

Results I and Results II established the oxide and nitride arms separately. This section places the two families on a single comparative footing using three axes that together characterize the behavioural domain-shift penalty: the zero-shot starting point, the fine-tuning response across training-set sizes, and the transfer benefit of pretrained initialization over from-scratch training. All comparisons in this section use the same pretrained formation-energy ALIGNN checkpoint [CITE: ALIGNN_PAPER], the same JARVIS `dft_3d` split logic [CITE: JARVIS_INFRA], the same canonical hyperparameter set (`epochs = 50`, `lr = 1e-4`, `batch = 16`), and the same fixed oxide and nitride test sets (1 484 and 242 structures, respectively). Parity-plot MAE values reported here are on-figure values computed from seed-averaged predictions and therefore differ slightly from the summary MAE values in Table `TAB_S1_FT_SUMMARY_BY_N`, which are computed as per-seed MAE averages.

### III.A. Zero-shot family gap

Evaluating the pretrained formation-energy ALIGNN checkpoint without target-family training on both family test sets isolates the behavioural starting point of transfer, before any target-family signal has been seen. Figure `FIG_ZS_COMPARISON` reports the same-checkpoint zero-shot MAE as 0.0342 eV/atom on oxides (n = 1 484) and 0.0695 eV/atom on nitrides (n = 242): the nitride error exceeds the oxide error by 0.0354 eV/atom and is 2.03× the oxide value.

The direction of the gap is consistent with the project framing that treats oxides as in-distribution relative to the broadly JARVIS-trained checkpoint and nitrides as a chemical-family out-of-distribution test [CITE: PROJECT_BRIEF]. The comparison is not attributable to a different checkpoint or a different evaluation protocol: both families are evaluated with the same pretrained model on fixed held-out splits produced by the canonical split logic. The test sets differ in size, so sampling uncertainty on the two zero-shot values is not identical; this affects how tightly each value is pinned but not the direction of the gap. Read conservatively, the comparison establishes a behavioural domain-shift penalty of approximately a factor of two in formation-energy MAE before any target-family fine-tuning, and characterizes the starting point from which each family's adaptation curve will be measured in §§III.B–III.C. This is a behavioural observation, not a mechanism claim: the zero-shot gap is compatible with several non-exclusive causes — distributional differences in bond chemistry, differences in pretraining coverage, or differences in intrinsic hardness of formation-energy prediction — and no causal attribution is made here. §IV introduces a correlational geometric counterpart that is consistent with the gap but does not explain it.

### III.B. Differential fine-tuning response across training-set sizes

We fine-tune the same pretrained checkpoint on each family at `N ∈ {10, 50, 100, 200, 500, 1 000}` labelled structures, with multiple seeds. For each family and each `N` we report mean test MAE and mean best epoch across seeds. The mean best epoch — the per-seed epoch at which validation loss reached its minimum, averaged across seeds — is a direct behavioural indicator of whether the model has entered a multi-epoch optimization regime or remained at an early-checkpoint fixed point.

The paired learning-curve panels (`FIG_S1_LC_OXIDE`, `FIG_S1_LC_NITRIDE`) and the accompanying summary (Table `TAB_S1_FT_SUMMARY_BY_N`) reveal three coupled patterns. First, at every `N` nitride mean MAE exceeds oxide mean MAE, with the family gap in mean fine-tuned MAE ranging from 0.0457 eV/atom at `N = 10` to 0.1257 eV/atom at `N = 100`. Second, oxide mean best epoch climbs from 1.0 at `N = 10` to 18.5 at `N = 50` and then to 20.0, 39.0, 39.0, and 35.5 at `N = 100, 200, 500, 1 000`. Third, nitride mean best epoch remains pinned at 1.0 across `N = 10, 50, 100, 200` and only departs from this value at `N = 500` (40.5) and `N = 1 000` (45.0). No nitride fine-tuning row beats nitride zero-shot MAE; the best genuinely-adapted nitride configuration at `N = 1 000` reaches 0.0907 eV/atom, still above the zero-shot value of 0.0695 eV/atom.

The family contrast is not only that nitride MAE is uniformly higher. It is that oxide begins genuine multi-epoch optimization by `N = 50`, whereas nitride remains operationally inert through `N = 200` — validation loss is minimized at the first epoch and does not improve over the fifty-epoch budget. The mean best epoch distinguishes these regimes more reliably than a single MAE value, because two models can have similar MAE for very different reasons: one because it has been optimized to a stable fit, the other because it has not yet moved away from its initialization. The nitride low-`N` rows should therefore be read as reports of the pretrained checkpoint at the first fine-tuning epoch, not as successful low-data adaptation; the clean cross-family comparison of adapted fine-tuning begins at `N = 500`, where both families show mean best epochs well beyond 1.0. Even at that size, and at `N = 1 000`, the family gap persists: the oxide endpoint lies close to oxide zero-shot while the nitride endpoint remains above nitride zero-shot. This is consistent with a picture in which the chemical-family shift is felt not only in the starting error but in how readily the same optimization protocol converts labelled data into adaptation.

We cannot rule out that a different hyperparameter schedule — a longer training budget, a different learning-rate schedule, or a more aggressive early-layer unfreezing policy — would push nitride fine-tuning into genuine adaptation at lower `N`. The canonical namespace used here is the project-fixed protocol and not the result of a per-family tuning search; no claim is made about alternative settings.

### III.C. Transfer-benefit contrast: pretrained versus from-scratch

From-scratch baselines were trained for both families at `N = 50` and `N = 500`. The transfer benefit at a given `N` is the difference between the from-scratch test MAE and the fine-tuned test MAE. No transfer-benefit claim is made at `N = 10, 100, 200`, or `1 000`, because no from-scratch baseline exists at those sizes in the canonical namespace.

Figure `FIG_TRANSFER_BENEFIT` reports the following. For oxides, from-scratch MAE is 0.5561 eV/atom at `N = 50` and 0.2643 eV/atom at `N = 500`; fine-tuned MAE is 0.0523 and 0.0430 eV/atom, yielding transfer benefits of 0.5038 and 0.2214 eV/atom. For nitrides, from-scratch MAE is 0.6914 eV/atom at `N = 50` and 0.3683 eV/atom at `N = 500`; fine-tuned MAE is 0.1173 and 0.0977 eV/atom, yielding transfer benefits of 0.5741 and 0.2706 eV/atom. Transfer-benefit bars are numerically larger for nitrides than for oxides at both tested sizes.

**Two critical interpretation caveats must be fixed before any cross-family ranking is read from the bar heights.** First, numerically taller nitride bars do not indicate more efficient transfer on nitrides: at both `N = 50` and `N = 500` the nitride from-scratch anchor is weaker and the nitride fine-tuned endpoint is worse in absolute MAE terms than the corresponding oxide quantities, so the arithmetic benefit is inflated by a worse scratch baseline, not by better adaptation. Bar-height ordering between families cannot be used as a measure of transfer efficiency. Second, the nitride `N = 50` bar is pretrained-initialization advantage over scratch, not low-data adaptation: the nitride mean best epoch at `N = 50` is 1.0, so the fine-tuned model at that size has not moved meaningfully away from the pretrained checkpoint. The `N = 50` nitride bar therefore measures the advantage of starting from that pretrained state instead of from random initialization; it does not measure successful target-family fine-tuning. Under the canonical protocol, the first clean cross-family comparison of adaptation benefit is at `N = 500`, where both families show mean best epochs well above 1.0. Scope is also limited to `N = 50` and `N = 500`; no from-scratch baseline exists at other `N`, so no transfer-benefit statement is made elsewhere on the curve.

With those caveats in place, two observations remain. Pretrained initialization outperforms random initialization in both families at both tested sizes, and the advantage is larger at `N = 50` than at `N = 500` in both families. This is qualitatively consistent with the general transfer-learning picture in which pretrained models retain value most strongly in the low-data regime [CITE: KIM2024; LEE_ASAHI2021]. Pretraining is therefore valuable for nitrides at the two scratch-tested sizes in the specific sense of outperforming random initialization by a large margin — a meaningful practical finding for small-data materials workflows, because starting from the pretrained formation-energy checkpoint is much better than training an ALIGNN from scratch on the target family in the low-data regime, even though, as §III.B shows, the nitride family still does not benefit from adaptation at low `N` and does not close the family gap at high `N`. The transfer-benefit comparison reported here is specific to the canonical hyperparameter set, the canonical JARVIS splits, and the two tested scratch sizes; no extrapolation is made to intermediate `N`, to other optimization schedules, or to other evaluation protocols.

### III.D. Endpoint parity comparison

As a visual endpoint diagnostic, we examine parity plots at the highest tested `N` for both families (`FIG_S1_PARITY_OXIDE_N1000`, `FIG_S1_PARITY_NITRIDE_N1000`), where both are in the genuinely-adapted regime (oxide mean best epoch 35.5; nitride 45.0). On-figure MAE values are 0.0383 eV/atom for oxides and 0.0829 eV/atom for nitrides. The oxide parity scatter is visually tight along the diagonal across the population; the nitride scatter shows larger dispersion, including tail structures at both the low and high ends of the formation-energy range. Even when oxide and nitride fine-tuning are matched on the canonical hyperparameter set and both are in the adapted regime, the parity gap persists — consistent with the behavioural pattern in §III.B, in which nitride fine-tuning reduces optimization inertness but does not close the family error gap. Parity dispersion is a visual indicator, not a compositional breakdown; a finer-grained decomposition of the nitride residual by composition, coordination environment, or structural prototype would strengthen the interpretation but is beyond the scope of this section.

### III.E. Bridge to Results IV

Across zero-shot evaluation, fine-tuning across `N`, transfer-benefit at the two scratch-tested sizes, and high-`N` parity, a single comparative picture holds: pretraining helps in both families, but it does not close the family gap for nitrides under the canonical protocol. §IV therefore asks a different question: does the frozen pretrained representation from which these outputs are computed contain geometric structure consistent with the observed penalty?

## Results IV. Embedding analysis and interpretation of domain shift

§III characterizes the behavioural penalty using test MAE, learning-curve shape, adaptation timing, and transfer-benefit arithmetic — all outputs of the model. §IV examines the internal representation from which those outputs are computed: the penultimate pooled features of the pretrained ALIGNN checkpoint, extracted with the model frozen and no target-family training. The goal is not to replace the behavioural comparison with an embedding-based one, but to test whether the frozen representation shows structure geometrically consistent with the behavioural penalty already established. Every quantitative claim in this section is computed in the raw 256-dimensional `last_alignn_pool` space; PCA, t-SNE, and UMAP panels are descriptive visual support only and are not the basis of any numerical claim.

### IV.A. Implementation

Embeddings are extracted from the `last_alignn_pool` layer, the pooled graph-level feature immediately after the ALIGNN stack and before the formation-energy regression head. For each structure, the embedding is a raw 256-dimensional vector. **All statistical claims in this section are computed in that raw 256-D space.** Two-dimensional projections (PCA, t-SNE, UMAP) appear as descriptive figures only; their axes, inter-cluster distances, cluster sizes, and apparent densities are not metric quantities and are not used to make numerical claims. t-SNE and UMAP in particular preserve aspects of local neighbourhood structure but do not preserve global inter-cluster geometry [CITE: WEEK2_ASSIGNMENT]. The PCA projection used in the main figure explains 18.13 % of variance on PC1 and 9.47 % on PC2 (27.60 % cumulative) when fit on a balanced pool of 4 092 structures, and is therefore informative but lossy as a 2-D summary of the 256-D geometry.

The family-separation analysis uses the fixed test set of 1 726 structures (1 484 oxides, 242 nitrides). The distance–error analysis uses the full nitride test set (242 structures) and an oxide-reference pool of 13 507 oxide train+val structures. For each nitride test structure, we compute the mean Euclidean distance from its raw 256-D `last_alignn_pool` embedding to its five nearest oxide-reference embeddings; this is the "5NN oxide-reference distance" used throughout §IV.C. Statistical tests report 95 % confidence intervals (bootstrap) and FDR-adjusted `q`-values.

### IV.B. Family separation in the frozen pretrained representation

Computed in raw 256-D `last_alignn_pool` space across the fixed test set (Table `TAB_EA_FAMILY_SEPARATION`), the overall silhouette score is 0.2392 (CI 0.2332–0.2456); per-family silhouettes are 0.2546 for oxides (CI 0.2476–0.2617) and 0.1453 for nitrides (CI 0.1316–0.1582). The Davies–Bouldin index is 1.8290 (CI 1.7340–1.9071). Local 15-nearest-neighbour family purity is 0.9655 overall (CI 0.9603–0.9708), with oxide neighbourhoods at 0.9872 (CI 0.9832–0.9906) and nitride neighbourhoods at 0.8331 (CI 0.7978–0.8645). A logistic regression trained to recover family labels from the frozen embeddings achieves an AUC of 0.9994 (CI 0.9984–0.9999).

Two raw-space patterns emerge. First, family labels are almost perfectly recoverable from the frozen 256-D embeddings (AUC 0.9994); the pretrained representation encodes chemistry-level information that distinguishes oxides from nitrides without supervision on that distinction. Second, the two families are not symmetric in raw space: per-family silhouette (0.2546 vs 0.1453) and per-family 15-NN purity (0.9872 vs 0.8331) show the nitride region as distinguishable from oxides but internally less cohesive than the oxide region. The PCA, t-SNE, and UMAP panels (`FIG_EA_6A_PCA`, `FIG_EA_6B_TSNE`, `FIG_EA_6C_UMAP`) are broadly consistent with this raw-space pattern and are included as descriptive visual support for how the structure appears under three standard projection methods. No claim about family compactness, asymmetry, or inter-family distance rests on the projections themselves.

The frozen pretrained representation therefore carries family-level structure before any target-family training, and the per-family asymmetry in raw-space cohesion aligns in direction with the behavioural asymmetry established in §III. Raw-space metrics and behavioural MAE curves are consistent: they describe the same contrast from two different measurement surfaces. The logistic-regression AUC quantifies family-label recoverability from the frozen embeddings, not the mechanism by which formation-energy prediction errors arise; a near-perfect family AUC is compatible with many downstream error patterns. The silhouette, Davies–Bouldin, 15-NN purity, and logistic-AUC values are raw-space geometric descriptors; they do not quantify how much of the behavioural MAE gap is attributable to representation structure as opposed to label distribution, prediction-head calibration, or other non-geometric factors. The 2-D projections, again, are descriptive only.

### IV.C. Error-linked distance: nitride zero-shot error versus oxide-reference distance

Within the 242 nitride test structures, we ask whether the nitrides the pretrained model predicts poorly (large absolute zero-shot error) lie farther in raw 256-D `last_alignn_pool` space from the oxide-reference pool than the nitrides the model predicts well. Two complementary views: a hard-versus-easy tail contrast and a full-sample continuous association (Table `TAB_EA_DISTANCE_ERROR_STATS`, panels `FIG_EA_6D_BOXPLOT` and `FIG_EA_6D_SCATTER`).

Defining "hard" and "easy" nitrides as the top and bottom 20 % by absolute zero-shot error (49 structures each), the mean 5NN oxide-reference distance is 4.5988 for the hard group and 3.7821 for the easy group. The hard-minus-easy mean gap is 0.8168 (CI 0.4746–1.1597, FDR `q = 0.000180`), and the hard-minus-easy median gap is 0.8729 (CI 0.4161–1.2864, FDR `q = 0.000300`). Across all 242 nitrides, the Spearman rank correlation between 5NN oxide-reference distance and absolute zero-shot error is 0.3428 (CI 0.2214–0.4597, FDR `q = 0.000129`); Pearson correlation is 0.2770 (CI 0.1741–0.3890, FDR `q = 0.000129`).

Both the tail contrast and the full-sample association point in the same direction: nitrides located farther from the oxide-reference region in frozen pretrained space tend to have larger zero-shot errors. The boxplot captures this as a clear effect-size difference at the tails, and the scatter shows that the relationship extends across the full nitride test population as a moderate positive monotonic and linear association. The 5NN oxide-reference distance is therefore a correlational geometric indicator of nitride prediction difficulty. Its distinctive contribution relative to §III is localization: test MAE and learning curves describe the behavioural penalty at the family level but do not localize relative difficulty to any representation-space feature, whereas the distance–error association co-varies with zero-shot error across the nitride population and therefore provides a within-family geometric correlate of that difficulty.

The relationship is correlational and non-deterministic. The hard and easy groups overlap substantially in distance, the Spearman rank correlation is moderate (0.3428), and the association is reported for absolute zero-shot error only — not for fine-tuned error, where the picture may differ. We do not claim that distance from the oxide-reference pool causes prediction error, that it is the only geometric factor associated with error, or that it applies symmetrically to oxides; the oxide-reference pool is an operational reference region defined from the heavier-represented family in the training data, not evidence that the pretrained checkpoint is oxide-exclusive.

### IV.D. What the embedding analysis adds beyond MAE and learning curves

The MAE tables and learning curves in §§III.A–III.D establish the domain-shift penalty as a behavioural property of the model: nitride zero-shot error is about 2× oxide zero-shot error, oxide optimization begins at `N = 50` while nitride optimization is delayed to `N = 500`, and the nitride family gap persists even when both families are in the adapted regime. Those results answer the question "how large is the penalty, and when does it appear?".

The embedding analysis in §§IV.B–IV.C answers a different question: does the frozen pretrained representation, examined before any target-family training, already show structure consistent with the observed penalty? It contributes three pieces of information that MAE tables and learning curves do not. First, family structure is already present in the frozen representation to the point where a linear probe recovers family labels almost perfectly (AUC 0.9994), bringing the representation itself — and not only the downstream regression head — into view as a geometric correlate of the domain-shift picture. Second, family structure is asymmetric in raw space: by every per-family raw-space metric considered, the oxide region is more internally cohesive than the nitride region, and that asymmetry aligns with the direction of the behavioural asymmetry. Third, the analysis provides a within-family geometric indicator — 5NN oxide-reference distance — that co-varies with zero-shot error across the full nitride population, supplying a geometric correlate of relative difficulty across structures that aggregate MAE cannot resolve.

None of these additions replaces the behavioural evidence, and none demonstrates causality. What the embedding analysis adds is a representation-space counterpart to the behavioural pattern, one that makes the domain-shift interpretation geometrically legible without upgrading it to a mechanism claim.

### IV.E. Synthesis of the geometric and behavioural evidence

Taken together, §§III and IV describe a consistent picture under the canonical protocol. The pretrained formation-energy ALIGNN checkpoint starts from a meaningfully higher zero-shot MAE on nitrides than on oxides, adapts slowly on nitrides relative to oxides across `N`, and does not close the family gap even at `N = 1 000`. In the frozen representation from which those outputs are computed, oxides and nitrides occupy family-distinguishable regions in raw 256-D space; the nitride region is less cohesive than the oxide region by every per-family raw-space metric considered; and within nitrides, zero-shot error co-varies with distance from the oxide-reference region. The behavioural penalty and the representation-space geometry are consistent, but the consistency is correlational rather than causal: the frozen representation is the setting in which the penalty is measured, not a proven source of it. The Discussion develops the practical implications of this combined picture for small-data transfer in materials property prediction.
